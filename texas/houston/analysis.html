Executive Summary & Macro Landscape

This section provides the critical macro-level context of the City of Houston for FY26. It highlights the primary administrative goals of the current mayoral administration, framing the high-level business drivers that ultimately fund technology procurements. Understanding these goals is essential for positioning Dell solutions as strategic enablers rather than simple IT commodities.

🏙

Political Climate

The current administration under Mayor John Whitmire focuses heavily on fiscal responsibility, public safety enhancement, and infrastructure resilience following recent severe weather events. There is a mandate to "do more with less" and consolidate duplicative agency efforts.

💰

Economic Climate

Houston maintains a robust economy transitioning from legacy energy to tech and healthcare. However, the municipal government faces a historical structural budget deficit, making CapEx requests highly scrutinized. Financing models (OpEx) are increasingly favored.

🚨

Core FY Goals

  • Public Safety Data Modernization (HPD, HFD)
  • Infrastructure & Flood Resilience (IoT, Edge)
  • Digital Equity & Broadband Access
  • Cybersecurity Zero-Trust Implementation

CIO & Director of HITS (Houston IT Services)

Technical Influence: High Direct Financial Power: Medium
Role & Focus: Centralizes enterprise applications, cybersecurity, and data center operations. Pushing for cloud-smart policies, cost-reduction through vendor consolidation, and strict cybersecurity compliance.
Governance Model: Hybrid/Federated. HITS acts as a service provider to departments. Constant friction regarding "Chargeback" models where departments feel HITS services are too expensive or slow, leading departments to procure their own solutions.
Dell AE Action: Position Dell APEX as a way for HITS to offer internal "cloud-like" consumption models to their departments, smoothing out the chargeback friction and keeping data on-prem/hybrid for cost control.

Mayor's Office

Technical Influence: Low Direct Financial Power: Very High
Role & Focus: Sets the annual budget priorities. Focused entirely on citizen outcomes: less crime, fewer potholes, faster emergency response, and lower taxes. Has no interest in IT feeds and speeds.
Current Pain Point: Structural deficit. The city cannot afford massive multi-million dollar upfront capital outlays without passing bonds.
Dell AE Action: Frame all large infrastructure proposals through Dell Financial Services (DFS) or APEX. Tie technology (e.g., edge servers) directly to outcomes (e.g., faster AI-driven traffic light optimization to reduce congestion).

City Controller

Technical Influence: Low Direct Financial Power: High
Role & Focus: The Chief Financial Officer of the city. Audits all departments, processes all payments, and certifies the availability of funds before City Council can approve a contract.
Current Pain Point: Ensuring compliance with federal grant spending (ARPA, Infrastructure Bill) and preventing procurement fraud or waste.
Dell AE Action: Ensure all Dell proposals utilize pre-vetted cooperative purchasing agreements (DIR, OMNIA) to guarantee rapid controller approval. Highlight ROI and total cost of ownership (TCO).

City Council (Tech & Finance Committees)

Technical Influence: Low Direct Financial Power: High
Role & Focus: Must vote to approve any technology contract over $50,000. They ask questions primarily about vendor diversity (MWBE compliance), local economic impact, and constituent privacy.
Current Pain Point: Approving large "black box" IT spending without understanding the citizen benefit. Highly sensitive to data privacy regarding AI and surveillance.
Dell AE Action: Partner with strong, certified local MWBE (Minority/Women-owned Business Enterprise) resellers in Houston. Council will reject direct Dell deals if MWBE participation goals are not met.

Agency IT Directors (HPD, Public Works, Aviation)

Technical Influence: Very High Direct Financial Power: High (within department)
Role & Focus: Enterprise-level departments like Houston Airport System (HAS), Houston Public Works (HPW), and Houston Police Department (HPD) have massive independent budgets and their own IT staffs. They dictate edge, IoT, and specific application infrastructure.
Current Pain Point: Waiting on central HITS for provisioning. Need massive unstructured data storage (video evidence, airport security, water meter telemetry).
Dell AE Action: Bypass HITS for specific edge/storage plays. Pitch PowerScale directly to HPD for body-cam video. Pitch Edge gateways directly to HPW for SCADA/water systems. These agencies have capital and avoid central IT bottlenecks.

    Mandated to create a completely isolated, immutable backup vault. Currently assessing vendors to replace aging legacy tape/disk backups that are vulnerable to network infiltration.

Procurement, Vendor Landscape & Compliance

Contract Vehicles

  • DIR (TX Dept of Info Resources): Primary vehicle. Mandatory for HITS unless a waiver is secured.
  • OMNIA Partners: Heavily used by HPW and HAS for infrastructure.
  • BuyBoard: Used for smaller commodity client purchases.

Compliance Mandates

  • CJIS: Critical for HPD data. Requires strict physical and logical access controls for storage.
  • StateRAMP: Houston is aligning with Texas StateRAMP guidelines for all cloud and SaaS vendors.
  • HIPAA: Health Department requires encrypted-at-rest data solutions.

Competitive Incumbents & Technical Debt

Houston relies heavily on a massive, aging Cisco UCS environment for central compute and legacy NetApp arrays approaching end-of-support. There is significant frustration with current public cloud (AWS/Azure) unbudgeted cost overruns. Endpoint security is fragmented across different agencies.
