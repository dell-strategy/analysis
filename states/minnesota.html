Minnesota runs on a two-year (biennial) cycle; the FY2026-27 budget signed by Governor Walz in June 2025 holds net general-fund spending to roughly $66B, so the live opportunity is mid-biennium execution rather than a fresh appropriation. Three shifts matter to a Dell seller. First, leadership turned over: Jon Eichten became MNIT Commissioner and State CIO on March 16, 2026, succeeding longtime CIO Tarek Tomes, who moved to a senior technology role at the University of Minnesota — putting a Dell-relevant buyer on both sides of that move. Second, a 2025 data-center tax law (Chapter 13) added a 'qualified large-scale data center' tier and extended the equipment sales-tax exemption to 35 years, helping pull a wave of hyperscale projects (Meta's ~$800M Rosemount campus is under construction; 20-plus more proposed) — though the state simultaneously ended the electricity exemption and the boom faces grid and local-opposition headwinds. Third, MNIT is running a Whole-of-State Cybersecurity Plan and a TAIGA AI-governance effort, both creating governed infrastructure and security demand. Buying runs through MNIT Procurement with the Department of Administration's Office of State Procurement, and cooperatively through the Cooperative Purchasing Venture (CPV).

Budget & Fiscal

Minnesota budgets on a two-year (biennial) cycle. The FY2026-27 budget was passed by the Legislature and signed by Governor Walz in June 2025, holding net general-fund spending to just under $66B for the biennium. There is no single 'state IT budget' line: MNIT, the central IT agency, is funded almost entirely through a chargeback model in which agencies pay for the services they consume, with only about 5% coming from direct general-fund and special-revenue appropriations. Technology spend is therefore distributed across agency budgets and a set of named technology, cyber, and broadband programs.

Initiatives & Policy

Hyperscale Data-Center Buildout4

Accelerating

A 2025 law (Chapter 13) created a 'qualified large-scale data center' tier (25,000+ sq ft and $250M+ invested within 60 months) and extended the IT-equipment sales-tax exemption to 35 years, pulling a wave of hyperscale projects. Meta's ~$800M Rosemount campus is under construction and 20-plus sites have been proposed statewide. The state ended the electricity exemption and projects face grid and local-opposition headwinds, but this remains the strongest tailwind for Dell AI-infrastructure and ISG demand in Minnesota.

Procurement & Competition

Minnesota does not centralize IT buying in a single contract authority the way some states do. MNIT's Procurement Division works with the Department of Administration's Office of State Procurement (OSP) to establish competitive vendor contracts for IT hardware, software, and professional services for executive-branch agencies. Many of those contracts are also extended to other public entities through the state's Cooperative Purchasing Venture (CPV).

Competitive landscape

Minnesota is a contract-driven, relationship-heavy market where incumbents hold state and cooperative agreements across compute, networking, and services. Differentiation comes from AI-infrastructure capability tied to the data-center and Mayo/UMN buildouts, governed-AI and security offerings that map to TAIGA and the Whole-of-State plan, and managed services for under-resourced local governments — rather than price alone.
