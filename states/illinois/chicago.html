Overview

Chicago is the third-largest city in the United States — roughly 2.7 million residents — governed by a strong-mayor system under Mayor Brandon Johnson with a 50-member City Council. Enterprise technology direction sits with the Department of Technology & Innovation (DTI), the renamed and reformed central IT agency that sets citywide technology strategy, runs shared infrastructure and networks, delivers cybersecurity, and manages major technology programs for City departments; it is led by a Commissioner who also serves as the City's Chief Information Officer. Chicago is best understood as a centralized-but-departmental buyer: DTI owns enterprise infrastructure, security, data, and platforms, while large operational departments (Police, the Office of Emergency Management & Communications, Aviation, Water Management, Streets & Sanitation, Public Health) carry their own systems and modernization needs. The defining backdrop for 2026 is fiscal: the City closed a projected Corporate Fund shortfall of roughly $1.1–1.2 billion and the City Council passed a $16.6B budget in December 2025 — the first in city history crafted by the Council rather than the mayor's finance team — leaving officials warning the result is thin, with forecasts pointing to another large gap (~$1.16–1.3B) for 2027. For a Dell SLG seller this is one of the largest single municipal opportunities in the Midwest; the entry point is DTI's enterprise programs plus the Department of Procurement Services' competitively bid and cooperative vehicles, with most material contracts requiring City Council approval — not a single discretionary IT wallet.

Budget & Fiscal

Chicago's 2026 budget was shaped by a large structural deficit. The City forecast a Corporate Fund gap of roughly $1.15B for 2026, and after the mayor's spending plan stalled the City Council passed a ~$16.6B budget in December 2025, which Mayor Johnson allowed to take effect without his signature. The pressure does not ease: forecasts point to another projected shortfall of roughly $1.16–1.3B for 2027, driven partly by rising pension costs (total pension liability reported at ~$36.4B). DTI's own appropriation is funded mostly from the Corporate Fund (~69% of its proposed 2026 budget) with federal grants the next-largest source (~16%); software maintenance and licensing is DTI's single largest expense category — a signal that the application and licensing estate, not new build, dominates the technology line.

Initiatives & Priorities

Generative AI roadmap & responsible-use policy

DTI, under the CIO, published a City of Chicago Roadmap for AI focused on generative AI, with guiding principles (equitable, inclusive, responsible) and recommended actions — a short employee acceptable-use policy, an AI center of excellence, and cross-department governance. A 2024 ordinance further directs AI programs under the CIO/DTI across areas like traffic, infrastructure, public safety, and constituent virtual assistants, with semi-annual reporting. The City now fields a First Deputy Corporation Counsel / Head of Privacy & AI, and city leaders participated in Chicago AI Week (June 2026) — an early, public lane for AI-governance, data-platform, and AI-infrastructure conversations.

Cybersecurity & enterprise resilience

DTI owns citywide information-security strategy across networks, enterprise systems, and infrastructure for a high-profile target — from O'Hare to regional water systems. The Information Security Office runs a shared-services model, and CISO Bruce Coffing has grown the security team from ~6 to ~24 budgeted positions since 2018 while framing AI as the major disrupter for both attackers and defenders. With a constrained budget and a heavy legacy estate, security operations, modernization, and resilience are a continuing, executive-relevant priority.

Digital equity & broadband

Chicago's Digital Equity Council published a community-led Digital Equity Plan, and the Chicago Connected program — estimated at ~$50M over four years — provides free high-speed internet/hotspots to tens of thousands of CPS students, prioritizing the South and West Sides. The City is also pursuing neighborhood broadband and public Wi-Fi using city assets. Device, connectivity, and endpoint demand at community scale, often grant- or partner-funded.

Legacy modernization & infrastructure refresh

DTI's budget points to an aging estate — rising IT-maintenance costs and a large legacy telephone (Centrex) line — alongside heavy software maintenance and licensing. The opportunity is consolidation, infrastructure refresh, and migration off legacy systems framed as cost takeout in a deficit year, not added spend.

Modernization Data Center

Procurement

Selling to Chicago means navigating central procurement and the City Council, not a single buying desk. Competitively bid supplies, equipment, and services run through the Department of Procurement Services (DPS), where the Chief Procurement Officer is the contracting authority and vendors register and bid via the City's eProcurement system. DPS administers master agreements for hardware, software, and technology services via task orders, and the City can use cooperative ('piggyback') contracts to speed sourcing. Material contracts require City Council ordinance approval — build that timeline into any forecast.
