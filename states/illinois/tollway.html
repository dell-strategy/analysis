Overview

The Illinois State Toll Highway Authority — the Illinois Tollway — is a self-supporting state special district that operates and maintains 294 miles of toll roads on five routes (I-90 Jane Addams, I-88 Reagan, I-355 Veterans, I-294/I-94 Tri-State, and the I-490/Elgin O'Hare Western Access corridor) across 12 northern Illinois counties. It is governed by an 11-member Board of Directors and run day-to-day by an Executive Director, with roughly 1,400 employees. Unlike most state agencies, the Tollway receives no general state tax revenue: it funds operations, maintenance, debt service, and capital almost entirely from toll collections and revenue bonds. That makes it unusually technology-dependent — the system is 100% all-electronic open-road tolling (cash collection ended in 2021), so I-PASS/E-ZPass transponder reads, license-plate (ANPR) cameras, the tolling back-office, intelligent transportation systems (ITS), and the operational technology (OT) along the roadway are mission-critical revenue and safety infrastructure rather than back-office IT. For a Dell SLG seller, the Tollway is a compute-, storage-, edge-, and cybersecurity-rich account whose buying is gated by board approval and the Illinois Procurement Code.

Budget & Fiscal

The Tollway's December 2025 board action set a 2026 budget of $1.79 billion in revenue covering operations, maintenance, debt service, and capital. Of that, $1.26 billion is capital and roughly $98 million is the Information Technology operating line — a sizable, self-funded technology spend that does not depend on state general revenue. A much larger commitment is queued: the proposed 15-year, $26.5B 'Driving Connections' program (2027–2042), which would be funded in part by the first toll increase since 2012.

Initiatives & Priorities

All-electronic / open-road tolling back office

The Tollway runs 100% cashless open-road tolling on I-PASS/E-ZPass plus ANPR plate imaging. The transaction back-office, image processing, and customer systems are revenue-critical and continually modernized — a natural fit for high-availability compute, storage, data, and analytics infrastructure.

Procurement

The Tollway buys under the Illinois Procurement Code, and contract awards require Board of Directors approval. Non-construction goods and services (where most IT/technology buys sit) flow through state procurement systems; construction and professional engineering follow separate IDOT/CDB and QBS rules. Vendors register and transact through the state's electronic procurement tooling.
