Overview

Louisville/Jefferson County Metro Government is the consolidated city-county government formed when Louisville and Jefferson County merged — the largest local government in Kentucky, serving roughly 770,000 residents. It is a strong-mayor government led by Mayor Craig Greenberg, with a 26-member Metro Council. Central technology runs through Metro Technology Services (MTS), the enterprise IT organization that delivers infrastructure, networking, applications, public-safety systems, GIS/smart-city, and cybersecurity across Metro departments, led by executive director (CIO) Chris Seidt. For a Dell SLG seller, Louisville Metro is best understood as a single, sizable municipal buyer with centralized IT and an unusually forward AI posture: in December 2025 the city named its first Chief Artificial Intelligence Officer (Pamela McKnight, formerly of Intel) and stood up a small AI office, then in January 2026 awarded its first AI pilot (permitting) via RFP. Cybersecurity has been led inside MTS for roughly a decade, and the city has a long-running smart-city program. Procurement is centralized in the Office of Management & Budget (OMB), and Metro can buy off cooperative vehicles (e.g., Sourcewell, NASPO ValuePoint via the KY participating addendum) in addition to its own competitive solicitations.

Budget & Fiscal

Louisville Metro is a direct end spender with a centralized budget, unlike the state cabinets — its leverage is its own ~$1.1B annual budget plus a centralized IT and procurement function. Metro Council approved the amended FY2026–27 (FY27) operating and capital budget of roughly $1.1 billion in June 2026 in a 22–3 vote, with public safety as the largest category and continued investment in housing, paving, and parks. MTS-specific line items were not separately confirmed here; treat IT as funded within the central budget plus dedicated AI dollars.

Initiatives & Priorities

Enterprise AI office & Chief AI Officer

Louisville named its first Chief Artificial Intelligence Officer (Pamela McKnight, ex-Intel) in December 2025 to lead an enterprise-wide AI strategy and a four-person AI team focused on responsible adoption across departments. A clear entry point for AI-infrastructure, responsible-AI, and workstation/compute conversations — engage the AI office and MTS together.

AI Leadership
AI permitting pilot (Govstream.ai)

In January 2026 Metro awarded its first AI pilot — via its first AI-focused RFP — to Govstream.ai to streamline residential permitting using assistants grounded in the city's own codes, spatial data, and permit records (aggregate fee cap ~$30K, running through spring 2026). A signal that Metro is moving from policy to procured AI use cases.

Cybersecurity & compliance

MTS has run Metro's cybersecurity, risk-management, compliance, and security-awareness program for roughly a decade (led by James Meece since 2015). Cyber also helped shape the city's AI policy. A standing lane for security, identity, endpoint, and data-protection offerings across a complex municipal estate.

Smart city & civic innovation

Louisville runs a long-standing smart-city / civic-innovation program inside MTS (open data, sensors, GIS, connectivity), with a long-term vision of fiber-to-the-home and environmental sensing. Relevant for edge/IoT data infrastructure, networking, and the compute behind data and analytics.
