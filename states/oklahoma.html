IT Strategic Plan
Cloud-native modernization

Oklahoma runs one of the most consolidated state IT models in the country: under the Information Technology Consolidation and Coordination Act (ITCCA), the State CIO at the Office of Management and Enterprise Services (OMES) controls planning, acquisition, and standards for technology — including AI — across 125+ executive agencies. That makes OMES the single most important account in the state. Three shifts matter for sellers in 2026. First, new leadership and strategy: Dan Cronin became State CIO in January 2025 and Tai Phan was named the state's first Chief AI & Technology Officer in November 2025, behind a FY2026–2028 IT Strategic Plan centered on cloud-native modernization and killing legacy systems. Second, AI and cybersecurity are now formal statewide programs, including AI-driven threat detection (Darktrace) and a published state AI-use standard. Third, a historic data-center buildout — led by Google's ~$9B Oklahoma investment plus Meta, IREN, and others — is turning the state into national AI infrastructure. Buying runs centrally through OMES Central Purchasing statewide contracts and cooperative vehicles (NASPO ValuePoint).

Where Dell can play
  • OMES as a consolidated enterprise account — statewide refresh, cloud-native modernization, and legacy-system retirement across 125+ agencies under one CIO.
  • AI-infrastructure / 'AI factory' demand tied to the Google, Meta, and IREN data-center boom (PowerEdge, networking, services).
  • Governed, secure AI adoption under Oklahoma's published AI-use standard — private/on-prem AI for sensitive state workloads.
  • Cybersecurity modernization tied to the state's AI-driven threat-detection program and Cybersecurity Awareness initiatives.
  • Endpoint, client, and lifecycle refresh standardized through OMES statewide and mandatory statewide contracts.

Budget & Fiscal

Oklahoma budgets on an annual fiscal-year cycle (July 1 – June 30). The Legislature's general appropriations bill (SB 1177) provides roughly $12.8B for FY2027, a slight increase over the ~$12.6B FY2026 budget, with new dollars for education, mental health, human services, and Medicaid. There is no single published 'state IT budget' line — technology spend is centralized through OMES and distributed across agencies, then standardized via statewide contracts and enterprise agreements the State CIO negotiates.

Initiatives & Policy

OMES IT Consolidation (ITCCA)4

In force

Under the Information Technology Consolidation and Coordination Act, the State CIO at OMES owns planning, acquisition, standards, and implementation of technology — including AI — across 125+ executive agencies. The CIO can designate statewide and mandatory statewide contracts and negotiate enterprise/consolidation agreements. This makes OMES a single consolidated enterprise account rather than many fragmented agency buys.

Procurement & Competition

Procurement is centralized at OMES. Central Purchasing runs statewide acquisition under the Central Purchasing Act, and the State CIO has separate authority to designate IT contracts as statewide — including mandatory statewide contracts that agencies must use. Cooperative vehicles (notably NASPO ValuePoint via participating addenda) extend reach. The consolidated model means a few correctly-scoped statewide contracts can cover most of the executive branch.

Competitive landscape

Oklahoma is a centralized, contract-driven market — winning means holding the right OMES statewide and cooperative agreements and aligning to the CIO's modernization plan. Incumbents and resellers (e.g., Carahsoft, WWT) hold cooperative coverage across compute, cloud, and services. Differentiation comes from cloud-native modernization fit, AI-infrastructure capability tied to the data-center boom, and governed-AI/security offerings rather than price alone.
