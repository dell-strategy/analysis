West Virginia is making an aggressive, infrastructure-led bet on the AI economy. The 2025 Power Generation and Consumption Act (HB 2014) created a statewide certified-microgrid program letting 'high-impact data centers' (50MW+ critical IT load) power themselves off-grid using coal, gas, and renewables — and it is already working: in February 2026 the state designated its first High Impact Intelligence Center, a $4B, 600MW Penzance campus in Berkeley County. The state budgets annually on a July–June fiscal year; the FY2027 budget Governor Morrisey signed in March 2026 is roughly $5.5B in general revenue, intentionally 'flat.' Centrally, technology runs through the Office of Technology (OT) under the Department of Administration, led by CIO Heather Abbott, who is driving a 'cloud-right, not cloud-first' modernization including an 18-month mainframe-exit, secure multi-cloud, and early AI pilots. HB 5638 (signed April 2026) consolidated cybersecurity authority under the CISO — and notably barred software-licensing terms that lock the state into a single deployment model. For Dell, the buying motion runs through OT IT statewide contracts and the Purchasing Division, with the data-center/microgrid buildout the strongest infrastructure tailwind.

Where Dell can play
  • AI-infrastructure demand tied to high-impact data centers landing under HB 2014 (PowerEdge, networking, storage, services) — beginning with the $4B Penzance/Berkeley County campus.
  • State IT modernization under CIO Abbott's 'cloud-right' strategy: mainframe migration, hybrid/private infrastructure, and endpoint/client refresh across agencies.
  • Consolidated cybersecurity program under the CISO (HB 5638) — secure compute, infrastructure, and managed services for an office an audit flagged as behind on its statutory framework.
  • Governed, on-prem/private AI for sensitive state workloads as OT moves from pilots (Gemini transcription/chatbots) toward production.

Dell Action Plan

  • Lead AI-infrastructure on the HB 2014 buildout: map Dell ISG / AI Factory offerings to the $4B Berkeley County intelligence center and pursue follow-on high-impact data-center sites as districts certify.
  • Engage OT on 'cloud-right' modernization — position on-prem/private and hybrid infrastructure for the mainframe exit and systems that shouldn't move to public cloud.
  • Bring a credible cybersecurity story to the CISO's office: secure compute, infrastructure, and managed services that help close the statutory framework gap the audit flagged (HB 5638).
  • Confirm Dell's coverage and reseller registration on OT IT statewide contracts (e.g. IP23 client/endpoint) and in wvOASIS VSS for the agencies you're targeting.
  • Lean into the HB 5638 anti-lock-in posture — differentiate with flexible, deployment-agnostic licensing and governed private/on-prem AI for sensitive state workloads.

Initiatives & Policy

HB 2014 — Certified Microgrid / Data-Center Program1

In effect (July 2025)

The 2025 Power Generation and Consumption Act created a statewide certified-microgrid program that lets 'high-impact data centers' (50MW+ critical IT load) operate on dedicated microgrids — independent of residential ratepayers — powered by coal, gas, and renewables, and sell up to 10% of generation wholesale. Site-certification rules were filed in November 2025. This is the single strongest tailwind for Dell AI-infrastructure and ISG demand in the state.

Announced (Feb 2026)

In February 2026 Morrisey announced the state's first High Impact Intelligence Center: a $4B, ~600MW (1.9M sq ft) campus by Penzance on 548 acres at the Bedington/Falling Waters site in Berkeley County, enabled by HB 2014 and HB 2002 permitting reforms. It proves the microgrid model can attract hyperscale AI capacity — a flagship greenfield AI-infrastructure account and a template for follow-on sites.

'Cloud-Right' IT Modernization (OT)6

Active

CIO Heather Abbott is executing the OT 2025–2030 Strategic Plan with a 'cloud-right, not cloud-first' philosophy: evaluate each system on cost/security/performance before moving it, exit the legacy mainframe within ~18 months, build secure multi-cloud, and pilot AI (Google Gemini for transcription, summarization, chatbots). Mainframe-exit and hybrid posture open doors for on-prem/private infrastructure where cloud isn't the right fit.

HB 5638 — Cybersecurity Consolidation4

Signed (April 2026)

HB 5638 consolidated oversight of state cybersecurity under the CISO (Leroy Amos) within OT, directing a unified statewide standards framework — and added an anti-lock-in amendment barring software-licensing terms that restrict the state's infrastructure-deployment flexibility. A legislative audit found the office had not yet implemented its statutory framework despite ~$1.3M in contracts, signaling real need for secure compute, services, and a credible delivery partner.

BEAD Broadband Deployment7

Approved (Nov 2025)

NTIA approved West Virginia's revised BEAD plan in November 2025: ~$545M across 142 projects to connect 73,044 unserved/underserved locations (down from a prior ~$959M plan under federal 'Benefit of the Bargain' reforms). Primarily a fiber-deployment program, but it underpins the connectivity and rural-health/telehealth modernization that surround the state's digital-economy push.

Procurement & Competition

Technology procurement runs through two connected bodies. The Office of Technology (OT), within the Department of Administration and led by the CIO, sets IT direction and maintains IT statewide contracts that agencies buy from; the Purchasing Division (also under Administration) manages statewide contracts, solicitations, and the wvOASIS Vendor Self-Service (VSS) registration that every vendor must complete. Active bids post to the West Virginia Purchasing Bulletin.

Competitive landscape

West Virginia is a smaller, contract-driven market where the action is shifting toward private data-center capital and federal broadband dollars. Differentiation comes from AI-infrastructure capability mapped to the HB 2014 buildout, a credible answer to the CISO's framework gap, and hybrid/on-prem options that fit a 'cloud-right' state — plus the HB 5638 anti-lock-in stance, which favors vendors who don't tie deployment to restrictive licensing.
