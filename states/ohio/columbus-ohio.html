Overview

Columbus is Ohio's capital and largest city, with roughly 910,000 residents, and central IT runs through the Department of Technology (DoT), which supports city departments, two data centers, and tens of thousands of endpoints. The account is defined right now by recovery: in July 2024 the city was hit by a Rhysida ransomware attack that forced a full systems shutdown, exposed the personal data of roughly half a million residents and employees (including crime victims and undercover officers), and left some systems offline for months. The aftermath has reframed DoT's agenda around cybersecurity, network segmentation, zero trust, and resilient infrastructure rather than greenfield smart-city work. Leadership is also in transition — long-tenured CIO Sam Orth retired in mid-2026, with Deputy Director Pam O'Grady serving as interim director while Mayor Andrew Ginther's office searches for a permanent successor. For a Dell SLG seller, Columbus is a large municipal account in active rebuild mode, where security-hardened infrastructure, endpoint, backup/recovery, and modernization carry unusual weight.

Budget & Fiscal

Columbus is a large municipal spender whose IT priorities have shifted decisively toward security and resilience since the 2024 attack. The clearest public signal is the $23M zero-trust network program City Council approved in 2025, layered on top of the city's annual operating budget and DoT's ongoing operations.
