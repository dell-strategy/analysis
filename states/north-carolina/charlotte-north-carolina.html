Overview

The City of Charlotte is North Carolina's largest municipality, with roughly 900,000 residents and a metro economy anchored in banking and finance. The city operates under a council-manager form of government: an elected Mayor and City Council set policy, and a professional City Manager (Marcus D. Jones) runs day-to-day operations across departments. Central technology sits with the Innovation & Technology (I&T) department, led by CIO and Director Markell Storay, which owns enterprise infrastructure, applications, data and analytics, cybersecurity, and the city's smart-city and innovation programs. Charlotte is a mature, ambitious technology buyer for a municipality — it has published a Technology Strategic Plan and a 2025–2028 AI Strategic Roadmap, runs the long-standing Smart Charlotte initiative, and operates its own cooperative purchasing program (the Charlotte Cooperative Purchasing Alliance) that other public entities can buy through. For a Dell SLG seller, Charlotte is best understood as a large, self-directed local buyer with its own procurement vehicles, layered on top of North Carolina's statewide and cooperative contracts — the city's own I&T leadership, not a state agency, owns most technology decisions.

Budget & Fiscal

Charlotte adopted a $3.6B balanced FY2026 budget (effective July 1, 2025) with no property-tax increase, alongside a FY2026–2030 Capital Investment Plan. The city does not publish a single headline IT figure the way the topline budget reads, so technology spend is best tracked through the I&T department budget and capital/technology line items rather than one number — confirm current figures in the adopted budget before forecasting.
