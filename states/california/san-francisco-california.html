Overview

The City and County of San Francisco is a consolidated city-county — a single government of roughly 870,000 residents that performs both municipal and county functions — led by an elected Mayor and an 11-member Board of Supervisors. Enterprise technology runs through the Department of Technology (DT, also branded SFTech), the city's centralized IT provider: it is led by the City Chief Information Officer, who also serves as DT's Executive Director, reports roughly 260 employees and an operating budget near $140 million, and delivers infrastructure, cloud, networking, public-safety radio, broadband, and cybersecurity to more than 30,000 city employees across dozens of departments. The CIO reports to the Mayor with functional reporting to the City Administrator. The defining context for the current cycle is fiscal stress under Mayor Daniel Lurie, who took office in January 2025: after a roughly $15.9 billion FY2025-26 plan, the city moved to a new ~$16.9 billion two-year budget (FY2026-27 / FY2027-28) that closes a ~$642 million deficit — largely by eliminating ~550 mostly-vacant positions, slowing hiring, reorganizing, and consolidating functions — and still faces structural gaps and federal-funding (H.R.1) risk in the out-years. Despite austerity, San Francisco is one of the most prominent municipal AI adopters in the country — it published citywide Generative AI Guidelines and rolled Microsoft 365 Copilot Chat out to nearly 30,000 employees. For a Dell SLG seller this is a large, sophisticated municipal opportunity, but one where the message must fit a cost-pressured, efficiency-first environment and route through the Office of Contract Administration and Chapter 21 purchasing rules.

Budget & Fiscal

For FY2026-27/FY2027-28 Mayor Lurie proposed a roughly $16.9 billion two-year budget that closes a ~$642 million deficit (and cuts the ongoing structural gap by ~$300 million); the Budget & Finance Committee advanced it in late June 2026 with a full Board of Supervisors vote set for July 21. The gap is closed mainly by eliminating ~550 mostly-vacant positions (with a small number of layoffs on top of ~127 previously announced), a multi-year hiring slowdown (~$130M), reorganization and attrition (~$80M), consolidation of city functions, and the city's first performance-based service contract — with $1B+ held in reserve against federal (H.R.1) cuts. This is an austerity cycle: out-year deficits and federal-funding risk persist. Technology spend is not one concentrated line — DT holds the enterprise IT appropriation (operating budget near $140M), but large departments (Public Health, MTA, the airport, public safety) carry their own substantial IT.

Initiatives & Priorities

Citywide Microsoft 365 Copilot rollout

After a ~2,000-employee, six-month pilot, San Francisco made Microsoft 365 Copilot Chat available to nearly 30,000 city employees at no additional cost under its existing Microsoft license — one of the largest local-government generative-AI deployments anywhere. A live lane for AI-infrastructure, data-readiness, endpoint, and security conversations, with a comparable municipal reference valued over decks.

AI Productivity
Generative AI Guidelines & AI governance

DT's Emerging Technologies team, with the city's AI Advisory Committee, maintains citywide Generative AI Guidelines (updated July 2025) built on transparency, no-deepfakes, and data-protection principles, with a risk-tiered approach and a tool inventory. The city has signaled a fuller citywide AI Strategy. Governance-forward posture means AI and data plays should lead with security, privacy, and compliance fit.

AI Governance
Cloud & infrastructure modernization (SFCloud)

DT runs SFCloud as a primary cloud option for city storage and compute and stood up a Cloud Center of Excellence (2023) to guide cloud adoption and migration across departments. An ongoing modernization track touching hybrid cloud, storage, data protection, and managed services — and a natural fit for ISG and as-a-service positioning.

Cybersecurity operations & broadband equity

DT's Office of Cybersecurity defends citywide systems under the City CISO, while the Fiber to Housing / broadband-equity programs extend connectivity to first responders and underserved communities. Security operations, identity, endpoint, and resilience are active spend areas, and connectivity programs add a network/infrastructure angle.

Procurement

Selling to San Francisco runs through the Office of Contract Administration (OCA, the city's Purchasing Division) under the City Administrator, governed by Chapter 21 of the Administrative Code — not a single buying desk. OCA administers hundreds of citywide term contracts for goods, services, and technology that departments buy from, and larger or sole-source deals carry additional approval steps. Vendors must register and meet city contractor-compliance requirements. Confirm the owning department first, then map the OCA/Chapter 21 path and any usable cooperative contract.
