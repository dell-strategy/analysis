Overview

The City of Los Angeles is the second-most-populous city in the United States — roughly 4 million residents — governed by an elected Mayor and a 15-member City Council. Enterprise technology runs through the Information Technology Agency (ITA), which serves as the city's central IT department: led by General Manager and CIO Ted Ross, ITA reports around 465 employees and delivers enterprise IT and digital services to roughly 45 city departments, tens of thousands of city employees, and the public. It is important to distinguish the City of Los Angeles from the separately governed County of Los Angeles; they are distinct buyers with their own budgets, CIOs, and procurement. The defining context for FY2025-26 is fiscal stress: the city entered the year facing a roughly $1 billion deficit (driven by overspending, liability payouts, soft tax revenue, and fire recovery) and adopted a roughly $14 billion budget that closed the gap through cuts, labor agreements that ultimately averted civil-service layoffs, and use of reserves. Despite austerity, ITA is a recognized digital-government leader (repeatedly ranked a top Digital City) and is moving aggressively on generative AI and on the SmartLA 2028 program tied to the 2028 Olympic and Paralympic Games. For a Dell SLG seller this is a large municipal opportunity, but one where the message must fit a cost-pressured, efficiency-first environment and route through city purchasing and Council approval.

Budget & Fiscal

The City Council adopted a roughly $15 billion FY2026-27 budget on a 12-1 vote in May 2026 with no major layoffs or furloughs — a notably calmer cycle than FY2025-26, which entered facing a ~$1B deficit and required labor deals to avert layoffs. The FY2026-27 plan still carries a projected shortfall (reported in the ~$200-250M range), which the city offset by raising reserves (General Fund Reserve to ~$515M, ~6% of revenues) rather than cutting staff, while funding 510 additional police officers and Inside Safe. Technology spend is not a single concentrated line — ITA holds the enterprise IT appropriation, but proprietary departments (DWP, Airports, Harbor) and large general-fund departments carry their own IT.

Initiatives & Priorities

Citywide generative AI rollout (Google Gemini)

LA moved from a ~100-user pilot to deploying Google Workspace with Gemini across its computer-using workforce (reported around 27,500 staff), backed by large-scale ITA training sessions (3,000-5,000 participants) and an eight-week 'super user' program. A live lane for AI-infrastructure, data-readiness, and productivity tooling, with comparable municipal references valued over decks.

AI Productivity
AI governance — LA A.I. Roadmap & Safety Checklist

ITA has published an 'L.A. A.I. Roadmap' and an 'A.I. Safety Checklist,' building responsible-AI governance, privacy, and security review into how the city adopts AI. Governance-forward posture means AI-infrastructure and data plays should lead with transparency, security, and compliance fit.

AI Governance
SmartLA 2028 ahead of the Olympics

ITA convenes the Smart City effort (SmartLA 2028) across dozens of departments to modernize connectivity, mobility, IoT, and digital services before the 2028 Olympic & Paralympic Games — a multi-year program touching network, edge/IoT, and data platforms. Mega-event readiness is a durable funding rationale even in an austerity year.

Smart City Modernization
Cybersecurity operations & resilience

ITA's security organization, under CISO Timothy Lee, defends against very high daily threat volumes and has modernized its SIEM and threat-prevention program. With the 2028 Games raising the regional threat profile, security operations, endpoint, identity, and risk-visibility are an active spend area.

Procurement

Selling to the City of Los Angeles runs through city purchasing and Council oversight, not a single buying desk. The General Services Department and departmental purchasing handle solicitations, vendors must register and comply with city contractor requirements, and contracts of significant value require City Council (and often Mayoral) approval. Cooperative contracts can accelerate catalog-style buys. Confirm the owning department first, then map the city-purchasing path and any usable co-op.
