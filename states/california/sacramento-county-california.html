Overview

Sacramento County is the local government for California's capital region — roughly 1.6 million residents — and one of the larger urban-county technology buyers in the state. IT is centralized: the Department of Technology (DTech) is the county's internal service provider for infrastructure, applications, networking, telecom, and security across county departments, with the CIO also serving as the County's Chief Information Security Officer. DTech is best understood as a shared-services shop that delivers to the county's operating departments rather than a standalone agency with its own large discretionary spend — the dollars sit with the departments it serves (Health, Human Assistance, Sheriff, Assessor, Voter Registration, etc.). The county runs a recommended budget of roughly $9B for FY2026-27, but that budget is under real pressure: a projected nine-figure structural deficit, $57M in general-fund reductions, and ~194.5 positions cut. For a Dell SLG seller, that combination — a centralized IT buying center plus a cost-constrained budget — means the motion leans toward consolidation, refresh efficiency, and modernization that demonstrably lowers run-cost, routed through the county's central procurement office.

Budget & Fiscal

Sacramento County's FY2026-27 Recommended Budget is about $9B all-funds (roughly $4B general fund), approved by the Board of Supervisors on June 10, 2026, with a Revised Recommended Budget due for adoption in September. The headline for tech buyers is the cost pressure behind it: a structural deficit (county officials cited ~$101M, about half attributed to federal changes), $57M in general-fund reductions, and ~194.5 FTE deletions. IT spend isn't broken out as a single public line — it flows through DTech's internal cost recovery and the budgets of the departments it serves.

Initiatives & Priorities

Property tax system modernization (TaxSys)

The county replaced a 1980s-era mainframe property-tax platform — which handles $3.1B+ in annual tax revenue across schools, cities, and special districts — with a modern system (TaxSys), live as of fall 2025. A reference point for the county's appetite to retire legacy and a model for the next mainframe-to-modern push.

Modernization Legacy Migration
Cybersecurity & CISO function

The CIO doubles as County CISO, consolidating security under DTech. Security assessments, penetration testing, and resilience are recurring procurement themes — a durable entry point for endpoint, identity, and infrastructure-security offerings even in a tight budget.

Cybersecurity
Infrastructure & data-center refresh

As a centralized shared-services provider, DTech carries ongoing server, storage, network, and end-user compute refresh across county departments. In a cost-constrained year, the pitch is consolidation and lifecycle efficiency, not greenfield expansion.

Data Center Refresh
Cloud & AI evaluation

Like most California counties, Sacramento is weighing cloud and emerging AI use cases within governance and budget limits. Treat AI as early-stage and tie any proposal to a concrete department workflow and a defensible cost case rather than a broad platform play.

Cloud AI

Procurement

Selling to Sacramento County runs through its central procurement office — the Department of General Services, Contract & Purchasing Services Division (CAPSD) — which issues solicitations (increasingly via an online procurement portal). Two paths shorten the cycle: the county's cooperative program and California statewide leveraged agreements that the county can buy off without a fresh RFP.
