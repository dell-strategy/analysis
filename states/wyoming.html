Wyoming is a small-budget state with an outsized AI-infrastructure story. It is the fourth-largest net energy producer in the U.S. and uses a fraction of what it generates, so Governor Gordon's 'Winning the AI Race' push is converting cheap, surplus power into a hyperscale data-center boom around Cheyenne — Meta's $800M+ campus, Microsoft's existing site plus a planned 3,200-acre expansion, and the Crusoe/Tallgrass 'Project Jade' campus starting at 1.8GW with room to scale to 10GW. The state also leads the nation on digital-asset law (45+ blockchain bills since 2016, SPDI banks, DAO LLCs, and the now-live Frontier Stable Token, FRNT). For Dell, the state's own IT spend is modest and runs through Enterprise Technology Services (ETS) under A&I via NASPO ValuePoint and statewide contracts — but the data-center buildout, the University of Wyoming, and the NCAR-Wyoming Supercomputing Center are where the real AI-infrastructure and HPC opportunity sits. ETS's 2026-2028 plan is centered on governance and cybersecurity hardening rather than big new programs, so security and modernization remain the central-government angles.

Where Dell can play
  • AI-infrastructure / 'AI factory' demand riding the Cheyenne data-center boom (Crusoe Project Jade, Meta, Microsoft) — PowerEdge, networking, services, and ecosystem pull-through.
  • University of Wyoming and the NCAR-Wyoming Supercomputing Center (NWSC) as established HPC/research-computing buyers with GPU refresh cycles.
  • Cybersecurity and risk-management modernization — the explicit top theme of the ETS 2026-2028 strategic plan for a lean central IT org.
  • Endpoint, client, and lifecycle refresh across executive-branch agencies on a tight biennial budget where value and consolidation matter.

Budget & Fiscal

Wyoming budgets on a two-year (biennial) cycle, with the main budget set in even-year general sessions. The 2025-26 biennial budget totals roughly $10.9B all-funds (about $3.4B general fund). There is no single consolidated 'state IT budget'; technology spend is distributed across agencies, and Enterprise Technology Services (ETS) sets rate structures that agencies use to build their IT ('400-series') budgets each biennium. Wyoming pursues digital transformation on a fraction of the budget larger states have — a structural reason value, consolidation, and shared services resonate. The 2025 supplemental budget did not pass, leaving some agencies strained heading into the 2027-28 cycle; Governor Gordon has since proposed an ~$11.13B 2027-28 budget.

Procurement & Competition

Wyoming's central IT organization is Enterprise Technology Services (ETS), with statewide purchasing run through the A&I (Administration & Information) General Services / Procurement Section. State agencies commonly buy IT off NASPO ValuePoint cooperative contracts (which satisfy Wyoming's competitive-bid statute) and A&I 'all-agency' statewide contracts. ETS also runs its own IT network-bid process for circuits/bandwidth. There is no single DIR-style co-op; the motion is leaner and contract-driven.

Competitive landscape

Wyoming is a lean, contract-driven market: most large IT buys flow through NASPO ValuePoint and A&I statewide contracts where incumbents already hold coverage. With a tight central budget, differentiation comes from value, consolidation, security alignment, and — above all — AI-infrastructure and HPC capability tied to the data-center boom, the university, and NWSC rather than chasing small agency price deals.
