Overview

The Kansas Office of Information Technology Services (OITS) is the executive branch's central IT organization, headquartered in Topeka and led by the Executive Chief Information Technology Officer (CITO). OITS delivers shared infrastructure — mainframe, data center (production operations in Overland Park, KS), network, and end-user services — increasingly on an 'as a service' consolidated model, and houses the Kansas Information Security Office (KISO) and a 24x7 Security Operations Center. Governance runs through the Kansas Information Technology Office (KITO), which supports the statutory Executive, Judicial, and Legislative CITOs, the Chief Information Technology Architect (CITA), and the IT Executive Council (ITEC) that sets statewide IT policy and standards. The defining initiative is the SB 291-driven IT consolidation: a multi-year effort (with Gartner Consulting on the assessment) to bring agency IT infrastructure and security under OITS to cut risk and improve cyber posture. For a Dell SLG seller, OITS is the executive-branch enterprise buying center, while the actual purchasing vehicles sit with the Department of Administration's Office of Procurement and Contracts.

Budget & Fiscal

OITS is funded through a mix of State General Fund (SGF), agency cost recovery for shared services, and federal dollars. The headline funding event is SB 291: a $15.0M SGF appropriation to stand up consolidated IT and security, layered on top of normal service costs. Initiatives & Priorities

SB 291 IT & security consolidation

The flagship effort: consolidate state-agency IT infrastructure and security under OITS to reduce risk and cost. Gartner Consulting led a current-state assessment running through October 2025, feeding a plan for 2026 integration projects. This is where infrastructure, security tooling, and managed-services demand concentrates — track which agencies migrate and when.

Consolidation Modernization
DCaaS & shared data-center modernization

OITS is moving the mainframe and data-center footprint (production in Overland Park, KS) toward a Data-Center-as-a-Service / 'as a service' model, with O365 and managed services. A direct opening for ISG infrastructure, hybrid cloud, and lifecycle/managed offerings.

Data Center As-a-Service
Whole-of-state cybersecurity (KISO)

KISO runs enterprise security for the state — a 24x7 SOC, baseline security tooling offered to agencies at no cost, and administration of the federal State and Local Cybersecurity Grant Program (SLCGP). Now formally extended beyond state agencies: SB 51 (signed April 2026) authorizes OITS to sell IT and cybersecurity services to political subdivisions — cities, counties, schools, hospitals, and nonprofits — on a chargeback model, opening a much larger local/whole-of-state audience. Security is a buying center in its own right and a consolidation priority under SB 291.

Cybersecurity Whole-of-State
Statewide generative AI policy

Under Gov. Kelly, OITS authored and the executive branch adopted a statewide generative AI acceptable-use policy (guardrails on restricted-use info, vendor disclosure of AI use). Signals AI governance is active — relevant for positioning AI-ready infrastructure and compliant solutions.
