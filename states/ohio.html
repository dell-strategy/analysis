ORC 9.64 via HB 96

Ohio enters the FY2026–27 biennium with a record ~$200B all-funds operating budget (HB 96, signed June 30, 2025) and a technology story dominated by one theme: AI infrastructure. Central Ohio has become one of the nation's densest data-center corridors — Google, Microsoft, Amazon, and Meta are all building — anchored by Intel's $28B 'Ohio One' semiconductor campus in New Albany. Two structural shifts matter most to sellers. First, the economics are being re-litigated: Intel has pushed its first fab to 2030–31, and in May 2026 Governor DeWine paused the state's data-center sales-tax exemption after it cost ~$1.6B in 2025 (12x the estimate), with the legislature studying a permanent change. Second, governance is tightening — DAS stood up a statewide AI Council and Generative AI sandbox, and HB 96 imposed new cybersecurity standards (ORC 9.64) on every local government effective September 30, 2025. For Dell, the buying motion runs through the Ohio Department of Administrative Services (DAS) and its State Term Schedules / cooperative purchasing, with Katrina Flory as the long-tenured state CIO.

Where Dell can play
  • AI-infrastructure demand across the central Ohio data-center corridor and university research computing (PowerEdge, networking, storage, services).
  • Statewide AI adoption under the DAS AI Council and GenAI sandbox — a chance to lead with governed, private/on-prem AI for sensitive state workloads.
  • Local-government cybersecurity uplift driven by the new ORC 9.64 mandate — endpoint, backup/recovery, and managed-security needs across 3,800+ subdivisions.
  • Endpoint, client, and lifecycle refresh across a record general-government budget and DAS enterprise shared services.

Dell Action Plan

  • Lead the AI-infrastructure conversation: tie Dell ISG / AI Factory offerings to the central Ohio data-center corridor and Ohio State / university research computing.
  • Engage local governments on the new ORC 9.64 cyber mandate — bundle endpoint security, resilient backup/recovery, and managed services for the 3,800+ subdivisions now required to comply.
  • Validate that Dell's DAS State Term Schedule coverage and reseller listings are current and correctly scoped, and confirm whether target categories are locked to a mandatory-use contract.
  • Position governed, private/on-prem AI for sensitive workloads as the DAS AI Council and GenAI sandbox move from pilots to approved use cases.
  • Track the Joint Data Center Committee and the tax-exemption pause — the incentive math for new builds is in flux, so time AI-infrastructure pursuits to where projects are already funded and underway.

Budget & Fiscal

Ohio budgets on a two-year (biennial) cycle running July 1 to June 30. HB 96, signed June 30, 2025, sets the FY2026–27 operating budget — roughly $60B/year in the General Revenue Fund and ~$200B all-funds across the biennium, the largest in state history. There is no single 'state IT budget' line; technology spend is distributed across agencies and delivered through DAS Office of Information Technology enterprise shared services, plus a set of named technology, broadband, and security programs.

Central Ohio Data-Center & AI Buildout6

Accelerating (with friction)

Central Ohio is one of the densest hyperscale corridors in the country — Google, Microsoft, Amazon, and Meta are all building near Columbus/New Albany. PUCO's 2025 AEP Ohio data-center tariff (large customers pay for 85%+ of subscribed energy) and a May 2026 pause on the sales-tax exemption add friction, but demand remains the strongest Dell ISG / AI Factory tailwind in the Midwest.

Intel 'Ohio One' Semiconductor Campus5

Delayed to 2030–31

Intel's $28B New Albany campus is the largest private investment in Ohio history, but the first fab has slipped to 2030 with production in 2030–31 and a second fab in 2032. Site work continues. The long horizon tempers near-term supplier demand, yet the campus continues to anchor Ohio's identity as a chip and AI-hardware hub and draw an ecosystem of suppliers and data centers.

DAS AI Council & GenAI Sandbox9

Active

The Ohio Department of Administrative Services established a statewide AI Council and a Generative AI 'sandbox' to vet use cases, set legal guardrails for third-party AI services, and build a central repository of approved GenAI use cases. Ohio also requires every K-12 school to adopt an AI policy by July 1, 2026. Agencies need governed, auditable AI — an opening for private/on-prem AI on sensitive data.

Local-Government Cybersecurity Mandate (ORC 9.64)10

In effect Sep 30, 2025

HB 96 added ORC 9.64, requiring Ohio political subdivisions to adopt cybersecurity standards, run risk assessments, train staff, maintain tested incident-response plans, and report ransomware incidents. With 3,800+ subdivisions now on the hook, this drives broad demand for endpoint security, resilient backup/recovery, and managed services — backed by the Ohio Cyber Reserve and Affordable Access to Cybersecurity aggregated purchasing.

Procurement & Competition

The Ohio Department of Administrative Services (DAS) is the central procurement and IT authority. DAS Office of Information Technology sets statewide IT direction and runs enterprise shared services (OAKS, the InnovateOhio Platform, Ohio Business Gateway). Most technology buys run through DAS State Term Schedules (STS) and Master Maintenance/Master Agreements; non-state entities, higher ed, and local governments can buy through DAS Cooperative Purchasing.

Competitive landscape

Ohio is a competitive, contract-driven market — incumbents hold DAS State Term Schedules across compute, networking, and services. Differentiation comes from AI-infrastructure capability tied to the data-center corridor, governed-AI offerings that fit the DAS AI Council, and local-government security/backup bundles that map to the new ORC 9.64 mandate rather than price alone.
