Maryland is modernizing aggressively while absorbing a historic fiscal squeeze. Governor Moore's FY2026 budget (~$67.3B) closes roughly a $3B structural deficit, so technology spend is being concentrated where it returns the most — centralization, legacy modernization, and AI-enabled service delivery. The Department of Information Technology (DoIT), led by Secretary Katie Savage, published a 2025 IT Master Plan (FY2026–2028) built on workforce, system centralization, and security, and stood up Maryland Digital Service, an AI Innovation Lab, and a high-profile Anthropic/Percepta AI partnership. The IT Investment Fund for Major IT Development Projects roughly doubled to $130M in FY2026, funding 58 projects. Maryland's defining advantage is cyber: Fort Meade, NSA, and U.S. Cyber Command anchor the nation's densest cyber ecosystem, reinforced by 2025 cyber legislation and a $1B 'Capital of Quantum' initiative with the University of Maryland. For Dell, the motion runs through DoIT statewide IT contracts and the eMaryland Marketplace Advantage (eMMA) portal, with cyber, AI, and modernization as the lead angles.

Where Dell can play
  • Legacy modernization across 58 funded Major IT Development Projects ($130M ITIF) — compute, storage, and services to retire aging state systems.
  • Cyber demand tied to Fort Meade / NSA / U.S. Cyber Command proximity plus 2025 state and local cyber laws — secure infrastructure, zero-trust, and OT/water-system protection.
  • AI-enabled service delivery (AI Innovation Lab, Maryland Digital Service, Anthropic/Percepta partnership) — governed on-prem and private-cloud AI for sensitive benefits and housing workloads.
  • Quantum and research-computing infrastructure via the $1B 'Capital of Quantum' initiative and the University System of Maryland.
  • Endpoint, client, and lifecycle refresh as DoIT centralizes duplicated agency IT under a single operating model.

Budget & Fiscal

Maryland budgets on an annual fiscal cycle beginning July 1. The FY2026 operating budget totals roughly $67.3B and was built to close one of the state's largest structural deficits in two decades — an estimated ~$3B gap — without raising sales or property taxes. There is no single 'state IT budget' line; technology spend sits inside DoIT's operating budget plus the IT Investment Fund (ITIF) that capitalizes Major IT Development Projects, with additional technology dollars distributed across agencies and named initiatives like quantum and broadband.

Initiatives & Policy

2025 IT Master Plan & System Centralization3

Active (FY26–28)

DoIT's 86-page 2025 IT Master Plan (FY2026–2028) is built on three pillars: modernizing the IT workforce (AI, cyber, data skills), centralizing duplicated agency systems under clear ownership, and strengthening security. It launched Maryland Digital Service and Maryland Benefits ('One Application'). Centralization and legacy retirement create direct demand for refreshed compute, storage, and services.

Procurement & Competition

Maryland's central technology authority is the Department of Information Technology (DoIT), which sets statewide IT policy, security, and standards and maintains statewide IT master contracts. Most state and University System procurements — including IT — are competed and posted through the eMaryland Marketplace Advantage (eMMA) portal, the state's and USM's mandatory bidding platform. DoIT statewide IT contracts (separate from but complementary to eMMA) let agencies buy from pre-competed vendor agreements.

Competitive landscape

Maryland is a competitive, contract-driven market with deep systems-integrator and federal-adjacent presence given the Fort Meade ecosystem. In a deficit climate, differentiation comes from modernization that lowers total cost (centralization, legacy retirement), demonstrable cyber/zero-trust credentials, and governed AI-infrastructure capability rather than price alone.
