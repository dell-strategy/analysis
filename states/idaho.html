Idaho is a small-population, fiscally conservative state, but two private-sector megaprojects are reshaping its technology landscape. Micron — headquartered in Boise — is building leading-edge DRAM fabs as part of a $200B U.S. expansion ($150B manufacturing + $50B R&D), with a second Boise fab announced in June 2025 and output targeted for 2027. Meta's roughly $800M hyperscale data center in Kuna — redesigned mid-build for AI workloads — is now targeted to come online by end of 2026. On the government side, the Office of Information Technology Services (ITS), under State CIO Alberto Gonzalez, is in the middle of a multi-phase statewide IT consolidation, and the FY2026 budget created a $10M Cybersecurity and Information Technology Resilience fund. Note that Idaho tightened spending mid-year: the governor ordered agency general-fund reductions in 2025 and signed additional FY2026 cuts in March 2026, so timing matters. For Dell, the strongest angles are AI/data-center infrastructure adjacent to the Micron and Meta buildouts and the consolidation-driven modernization of state agency IT, with procurement running primarily through NASPO ValuePoint cooperative contracts gated by the State CIO's office.

Where Dell can play
  • AI-infrastructure and data-center demand in the orbit of Micron's Boise fab expansion and Meta's Kuna hyperscale campus (PowerEdge, networking, ISG, services).
  • Statewide IT consolidation under ITS — a multi-agency centralization that drives infrastructure standardization, refresh, and managed-services needs.
  • The $10M Cybersecurity and Information Technology Resilience fund and rising cyber priority across state and local government.
  • Endpoint, client, and lifecycle refresh as consolidated agencies standardize on common platforms.

Dell Action Plan

  • Lead with AI-infrastructure: position Dell ISG / AI Factory offerings against the gravity of Micron's Boise fabs and Meta's Kuna data center, including university research-computing buyers.
  • Map to the ITS consolidation — bring standardized infrastructure, refresh, and managed-services proposals to a centralizing buyer rather than agency-by-agency.
  • Engage the State CIO's office early; cloud and major IT decisions for executive-branch agencies require its approval and the consolidation concentrates authority there.
  • Confirm Dell's NASPO ValuePoint coverage and Idaho participating addenda / resellers are current and correctly scoped for target agencies.
  • Tie secure-infrastructure and lifecycle offers to the $10M Cyber & IT Resilience fund and position governed, private/on-prem AI as agencies operationalize AI guidance.

Budget & Fiscal

Idaho budgets annually on a July–June fiscal year. There is no single consolidated 'state IT budget'; technology spend is distributed across agencies plus the Office of Information Technology Services (ITS), which is largely funded through service billings to the agencies it supports. The FY2026 general fund landed near $5.3B. Fiscal conditions tightened through the cycle: the governor directed agency general-fund reductions in 2025 and signed additional FY2026 cuts (SB 1331) in March 2026 — confirm current appropriations before forecasting.

Initiatives & Policy

Micron Boise Fab Expansion2

Under construction

Micron, headquartered in Boise, is building leading-edge memory (DRAM) capacity as part of a $200B U.S. expansion ($150B manufacturing + $50B R&D). A first southeast-Boise fab is completing around 2026; a second Boise fab announced in June 2025 targets output in 2027, alongside a $15B R&D facility. The cluster anchors a semiconductor and supplier ecosystem and is the strongest tailwind in the state for AI-infrastructure and ISG demand.

Meta Kuna Hyperscale Data Center4

Nearing operation

Meta's roughly $800M, ~1M sq ft data center in Kuna is its first in Idaho, backed by 100% renewable energy on Idaho Power's system and ~$70M in donated city water/sewer infrastructure. The facility was redesigned mid-build to support AI workloads at scale, pushing completion to end of 2026 (about a year later than planned); building exteriors are complete and the site hit peak construction (~1,400 workers) in early 2026. It signals Idaho as a viable hyperscale and AI-infrastructure location and seeds adjacent compute, networking, and services demand.

Statewide IT Consolidation (ITS)7

In progress

Under State CIO Alberto Gonzalez (appointed 2022), ITS has centralized IT services for more than 50 agencies. The final and largest agency, the Idaho Department of Health & Welfare (IDHW), reached its consolidation cutover on June 8, 2026 — effectively completing the multi-year agency-consolidation program (some integration/standardization tail work continues). Beyond consolidation, AI, privacy, ADA compliance, and citizen engagement are the CIO's 2026 priorities. Consolidation standardizes infrastructure and creates openings for refresh, common platforms, and managed services, but procurement decisions concentrate under the State CIO's office.

Cybersecurity & IT Resilience Fund1

Funded FY2026

The FY2026 budget created a $10M Cybersecurity and Information Technology Resilience fund covering IT line-item requests, equipment replacement, and infrastructure centralization. It is modest in size but signals security and resilience as a rising priority across state and local government — a hook for secure infrastructure and lifecycle offers tied to the consolidation.

State Government AI Rollout14

Early / governed

ITS published its risk-based 'Idaho AI Advantage' framework (eight core principles; risk-classification model gating high- vs. low-risk systems) to enable common, governed AI use across state government, with continued briefings to the governor's office and the legislative AI committee into 2026. Early use cases include a public HR chatbot with the Division of Human Resources, and a March 2026 public-private AI-in-education partnership. Idaho also passed targeted AI laws in 2024 (deepfakes, election integrity, CSAM). Agencies need governed, auditable AI — an opening for private/on-prem AI on sensitive workloads.

Procurement & Competition

Idaho's central buying authority is the Division of Purchasing (Department of Administration), while the Office of Information Technology Services (ITS) and the State CIO's office govern technology and cloud decisions for executive-branch agencies. Most technology buys leverage cooperative master agreements — chiefly NASPO ValuePoint (formerly WSCA-NASPO) — which are open to state agencies, higher education, and political subdivisions, often via participating addenda issued by the Division of Purchasing.

Competitive landscape

Idaho is a cooperative-driven, value-conscious market. Differentiation comes less from price and more from NASPO-ready offerings, AI-infrastructure capability adjacent to the Micron and Meta buildouts, and services that map cleanly onto the ITS consolidation. State CIO cloud approval and the consolidation's centralization mean fewer, larger decisions — engage ITS and the CIO's office directly.
