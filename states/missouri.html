Missouri enters FY2026 under first-term Governor Mike Kehoe with a $50.8B operating budget and an explicit message that 'spending, not revenue, is the problem' — meaning sellers should expect cost-justified, efficiency-framed buys rather than open-ended expansion. Three threads matter most. First, Google's $15B AI data-center campus in New Florence (announced May 2026) — backed by 1+ gigawatt of new generation — makes Missouri a national AI-infrastructure story and reshapes the energy and economic-development conversation. Second, Executive Order 26-02 (Jan 2026) directs the Office of Administration to deliver a statewide AI integration framework by November 30, 2026, opening the door to governed government AI. Third, the buying motion is centralized: OA-ITSD (~1,000 staff) runs IT for 15 consolidated executive departments, and the OA Division of Purchasing runs the statewide-contract and cooperative-procurement vehicles every deal flows through. For Dell, the play is AI-ready infrastructure, a client/endpoint refresh framed on efficiency, and getting onto (or validating coverage on) Missouri statewide contracts early.

Where Dell can play
  • AI-infrastructure demand riding the Google New Florence buildout and statewide AI-framework momentum (PowerEdge, networking, services).
  • Governed, on-prem / private-cloud AI for agencies operationalizing the EO 26-02 framework on sensitive state data.
  • Endpoint, client, and lifecycle refresh across 15 OA-ITSD-consolidated departments, framed on the administration's efficiency mandate.
  • Cybersecurity modernization tied to SLCGP whole-of-state grants and OA-ITSD Information Security.

Dell Action Plan

  • Lead with AI-ready infrastructure: tie Dell ISG / AI Factory to the Google New Florence buildout, the EO 26-02 framework, and University of Missouri research-computing demand.
  • Engage OA-ITSD as the consolidated buyer for 15 executive departments — server, storage, networking, and client/lifecycle, framed on the administration's efficiency mandate.
  • Validate Dell's coverage on Missouri statewide contracts and the cooperative program before pursuing deals; confirm resellers are current and correctly scoped.
  • Position governed, private/on-prem AI for sensitive state workloads ahead of the November 2026 AI framework deadline.
  • Map cybersecurity offerings to SLCGP whole-of-state grants and OA-ITSD Information Security for state-and-local endpoint and infrastructure modernization.

Budget & Fiscal

Missouri budgets on an annual fiscal-year cycle beginning July 1. Governor Kehoe signed the $50.8B FY2026 operating budget (about $15.4B in general revenue) on June 30, 2025, issuing more than 200 line-item vetoes to constrain spending. There is no single published 'state IT budget' line — technology spend is embedded across agencies and delivered centrally through OA-ITSD. The FY2027 cycle is shaped by a December 2025 consensus revenue estimate of $13.65B net general revenue (3.8% growth), with the administration signaling continued spending discipline.

In progress — due Nov 30, 2026

The order also tasks DNR and the PSC with addressing data-center energy demand. Agencies will need governed, auditable AI — an opening for private/on-prem AI on sensitive workloads.

Procurement & Competition

Missouri's technology procurement is centralized under the Office of Administration. The OA Division of Purchasing competitively bids and maintains Statewide Contracts — 150+ categories and 650+ individual contracts — that all executive agencies use and that are extended to local governments and quasi-public bodies through the Cooperative Procurement Program. OA-ITSD is the consolidated IT delivery arm that operationalizes these buys for 15 executive departments.

Competitive landscape

Missouri is a centralized, contract-driven, cost-conscious market. With buying concentrated in OA-ITSD and the Division of Purchasing, incumbents on statewide contracts hold an advantage, and the Kehoe administration's efficiency posture rewards clear ROI. Differentiation comes from AI-infrastructure capability tied to the data-center buildout, governed-AI readiness for the EO 26-02 framework, and lifecycle/TCO economics rather than price alone.
