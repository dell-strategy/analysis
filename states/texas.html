Texas enters the 2026–27 biennium with the largest budget in its history ($337.4B) and three structural shifts every SLG seller should know. First, cybersecurity is being pulled out of DIR into a brand-new standalone agency — the Texas Cyber Command in San Antonio — changing where security dollars and decisions sit. Second, the Texas Responsible AI Governance Act (TRAIGA) took effect January 1, 2026, putting real guardrails on government use of AI and creating an AI Advisory Council and regulatory sandbox. Third, an historic semiconductor and data-center buildout (TSIF, Meta, Stargate, TI) is turning Texas into national AI infrastructure. For Dell, the buying motion still runs through DIR Cooperative Contracts, and TX-RAMP cloud certification remains a hard gate on anything touching state data.

Where Dell can play
  • AI-infrastructure / 'AI factory' demand tied to the Texas data-center and semiconductor boom (PowerEdge, networking, services).
  • Stand-up of the Texas Cyber Command in San Antonio — secure compute, infrastructure, and managed services for a greenfield agency.
  • Agency AI adoption under TRAIGA guardrails — a chance to lead with governed, on-prem and private-cloud AI for sensitive workloads.
  • Endpoint, client, and lifecycle refresh across a record general-government budget.

Dell Action Plan

  • Lead the AI-infrastructure conversation: tie Dell ISG / AI Factory offerings to the Texas data-center and semiconductor buildout and the university research-computing buyers.
  • Engage the Texas Cyber Command early as a greenfield account in San Antonio — secure compute, infrastructure, and managed services for a brand-new agency.
  • Clear the TX-RAMP gate before pursuing cloud/as-a-service deals — confirm the right certification level for any offering touching state data.
  • Validate that Dell's DIR Cooperative Contract coverage and resellers are current and correctly scoped for the agencies you're targeting.
  • Position governed, private/on-prem AI for sensitive workloads as agencies operationalize TRAIGA.

Budget & Fiscal

Texas budgets on a two-year (biennial) cycle. The 89th Legislature's SB 1 appropriates $337.4B for 2026–27 — roughly $176B in FY2026 and $160B in FY2027 — the largest in state history. There is no single 'state IT budget' line; technology spend is distributed across agencies and a set of named technology and security initiatives funded this biennium.

Initiatives & Policy

Texas Cyber Command3

Launched

HB 150 (signed June 2025) created the nation's largest state cyber agency, headquartered in San Antonio with UTSA as a partner and led by retired Navy Vice Adm. Timothy 'T.J.' White. It is absorbing DIR's cybersecurity functions in phases — DIR transferred statewide cybersecurity employees and functions on March 1, 2026, with the transition slated to complete by Dec 31, 2026 and people/process/mission integration finalized by Feb 1, 2027 — and stands up a Cyber Threat Intelligence Center, Incident Response Unit, and Digital Forensic Lab serving state and local government. Active hiring for analysts and unit leadership through mid-2026. A greenfield agency means greenfield infrastructure, secure compute, and services needs.

TX-RAMP Cloud Security Program6

Mandatory

Under DIR, Texas agencies and public higher-ed may only contract for cloud services that hold TX-RAMP certification (NIST 800-53 Low/Moderate baselines). Certification must be maintained for the life of the contract. Any Dell cloud/as-a-service offering touching state data needs the right TX-RAMP level — a gate to clear early, not at close.

Procurement & Competition

The Texas Department of Information Resources (DIR) is the central technology procurement authority. Most state, local, and higher-ed technology buys run through DIR Cooperative Contracts, which let eligible entities purchase from pre-competed vendor agreements without running their own RFP. DIR also delivers Shared Technology Services and Communications Technology Services.

Competitive landscape

Texas is a competitive, contract-driven market — incumbents hold DIR agreements across compute, networking, and services. Differentiation tends to come from TX-RAMP-ready offerings, AI-infrastructure capability, and services that map to the Cyber Command and data-center buildout rather than price alone.
