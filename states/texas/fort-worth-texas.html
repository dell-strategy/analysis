Overview

Fort Worth is among the largest and fastest-growing cities in the country — consistently ranked the 11th- or 12th-most-populous US city and now approaching a population of roughly 1 million. The city government runs on a council-manager structure: an elected mayor and council set policy, and a professional City Manager (Jay Chapa, sworn in early 2025) runs day-to-day operations across departments. Technology is centralized in the IT Solutions Department (ITS), which delivers infrastructure, applications, networking, public-safety systems, and cybersecurity for city operations. Fort Worth adopted a ~$3.09B all-funds budget for FY2026 (a ~10.8% increase), of which the General Fund is ~$1.11B — the pool that funds most public-facing services. As of mid-2026 the city is forecasting a ~$49M General Fund shortfall for FY2027, which will tighten discretionary technology spend even as growth, AI adoption, and the regional data-center boom raise demand. Fort Worth buys most IT through Texas cooperative contracts rather than running every purchase as a standalone RFP.

Initiatives & Priorities

Citywide AI & GenAI program

Fort Worth adopted its first Generative AI policy in 2023 and expanded it in 2025 into a comprehensive AI/GenAI policy with a governance charter, board, and review committee. Reported use spans 18 of 27 departments — 911 call triage/translation, pavement and storm-drain assessment, HR, and legal research. AI-ready compute, data, and endpoint refresh are downstream needs.

AI Modernization
Cybersecurity for a growing city

ITS owns security operations for a large municipality and its public-safety systems. Local government remains a top ransomware target nationally, keeping endpoint, identity, backup/recovery, and resilient infrastructure on the agenda — a steady refresh-and-harden motion.

Cybersecurity Resilience
Infrastructure & cloud modernization

Sustained population growth drives demand for data-center, server, storage, network, and hybrid-cloud capacity to keep city services online. Tie infrastructure refresh to service-continuity and the cost pressures of the FY2027 budget gap.

Data Center Cloud
DFW data-center / AI buildout (regional context)

Dallas–Fort Worth is one of the largest US data-center and AI-infrastructure markets, with multibillion-dollar campuses (CyrusOne, Wistron AI plants, Siemens) and inventory projected to more than double. This shapes the workforce, power, and tech ecosystem around the city even though the campuses are private builds, not city procurement.

Procurement

Fort Worth centralizes buying through its Purchasing Division (Finance) and leans heavily on Texas cooperative contracts, which satisfy competitive-bid requirements without a standalone RFP. For a Dell seller the first move is confirming coverage on the co-ops the city actually uses and registering in the city's supplier portal.
