Overview

Travis County is the fifth-most-populous county in Texas, home to roughly 1.36 million residents and the City of Austin — the state capital and one of the country's most concentrated technology economies. Its central technology organization is the Information Technology Services (ITS) department, whose stated mission is to "execute, secure, and sustain technology services that benefit Travis County residents" — covering enterprise applications, infrastructure, end-user services, web services, and cybersecurity across county offices and departments. Governance runs through a five-member Commissioners Court (the County Judge plus four precinct Commissioners), which adopts the budget and approves contracts of size; the County Judge serves as chief administrator and Director of Emergency Management. Department-level executives carry the title "County Executive" (for example, the County Executive for Planning & Budget), and the Planning & Budget Office prepares the budget and integrates county-wide strategic and spending plans. For a Dell SLG seller, the practical map is: ITS is the technology buying center, the Planning & Budget Office shapes what gets funded, and Commissioners Court is the approval gate every significant deal must clear. Austin's position as a tech, semiconductor, and AI-policy center raises both the security expectations and the partnership options around the county.

Budget & Fiscal

Travis County's Commissioners Court adopted its FY2026 budget on September 23, 2025, balanced at a property-tax rate of 37.5845 cents per $100 of taxable value — a roughly 3-cent increase over FY2025, near the voter-approval rate. The county judge's office describes the Commissioners Court as overseeing a roughly $2 billion annual budget. Technology is a focused line within that, delivered through the ITS department; the fiscal year begins October 1.

Initiatives & Priorities

County-Wide IT Strategic Plan

ITS describes itself as collaborating across departments on a County-Wide IT Strategic Plan, optimizing service delivery and standardizing how technology is delivered to county offices. This planning track is the entry point for consolidation, infrastructure refresh, and managed-services positioning aligned to stated county priorities.

Modernization Strategy
T.I.M.E. portfolio rationalization

ITS applies a "T.I.M.E." framework — deciding whether to Tolerate, Invest in, Migrate from, or Eliminate systems based on business value and performance. This is an explicit gate for application and infrastructure decisions; align refresh and as-a-service proposals to the "invest" and "migrate" categories.

Optimization Infrastructure
Cybersecurity & secure-access posture

ITS commits to "anytime, anywhere" access to information that is "always available, accurate, and secure," and the county recruits for a dedicated Chief Information Security Officer role — a recurring opportunity for endpoint protection, CJIS-aligned security for justice systems, and resilience. Confirm the current security lead before engaging.

Cybersecurity CJIS
AI governance under Texas TRAIGA

Texas's Responsible Artificial Intelligence Governance Act (TRAIGA) takes effect January 1, 2026, setting a statewide framework for government AI use. As an Austin-based public entity, Travis County must align any AI tooling to this regime — an early governance gate that shapes how AI-enabled offerings can be sold in.

Procurement

Travis County buys through Travis County Purchasing, with awards of size approved by Commissioners Court. As a local government it is also eligible to buy off pre-competed cooperative vehicles — most importantly the Texas DIR Cooperative Contracts — which let it acquire technology at state pricing without running a full county RFP. Confirm Dell's coverage on the right vehicles before pursuing a deal.
