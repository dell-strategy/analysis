Overview

Harris County is the third-most-populous county in the U.S. and the largest in Texas, serving roughly 4.8 million residents across the Houston metro. Its central technology organization is Harris County Universal Services (HCUS), which runs IT, cybersecurity, telecommunications, radio, fleet, records management, and the county 311 line through eleven divisions, supporting around 18,000 county employees and roughly 70 departments. Governance sits with a five-member Commissioners Court — the County Judge plus four precinct Commissioners — which adopts the budget and must approve essentially every significant purchase. Day-to-day administration runs through the County Administrator's office, created in 2021. For a Dell SLG seller, the practical map is: HCUS is the technology buying center, the Office of County Administration and Office of Management & Budget shape what gets funded, and Commissioners Court is the gate every deal of size must clear. The county is mid-modernization — replacing its aging JWEB justice platform, standardizing infrastructure, and rationalizing software licensing — which is where ISG, client, and services opportunity concentrates.

Budget & Fiscal

Harris County adopted a roughly $2.8B FY2026 budget on September 24, 2025, after a contested process to close an estimated ~$200M gap. Technology is a comparatively small but strategically concentrated line: HCUS's proposed FY26 budget was about $96.1M, including dedicated cybersecurity, a large Microsoft licensing increase, and an EV-fleet pilot. The fiscal year begins October 1.

Initiatives & Priorities

JWEB → integrated justice platform (iJIMS)

Harris County is modernizing JWEB, the central judicial system serving 100+ courts and agencies (Sheriff, DA, Public Defender, Juvenile Probation, Pretrial). An RFI (No. MAY71425) for an Integrated Justice Information System closed July 29, 2025, targeting better interagency data sharing, reliability, and CJIS-compliant security. Large justice-infrastructure and data play.

Procurement

Harris County buys through the Harris County Purchasing Department, and any award of size must be approved by Commissioners Court. The county is an active user of cooperative vehicles — including Texas DIR Cooperative Contracts — which let it buy pre-competed technology at state pricing without running its own full RFP. Confirm Dell's coverage on the right co-ops before pursuing a deal.
