Overview

Dallas County is the second-most-populous county in Texas, serving roughly 2.6 million residents across Dallas and surrounding communities. Like most large Texas counties it is governed by a five-member Commissioners Court — a County Judge elected at large plus four district commissioners — which sets the budget and approves major technology purchases. Central technology is delivered by the county's IT Services department, which supports county-wide infrastructure, the justice and public-safety systems, elections technology, health and human services, and resident-facing digital services. The county's technology priorities were reshaped by an October 2023 ransomware incident (attributed to the 'Play' group) that exposed data on roughly 200,000 individuals; the county has since increased IT funding and, in December 2024, named a permanent CIO after more than a year without one. For a Dell SLG seller, Dallas County is a large, centralized county buyer with active cybersecurity, modernization, and resilience drivers — and one that purchases heavily through cooperative contracts rather than one-off RFPs.

Budget & Fiscal

Dallas County adopts an annual all-funds operating and capital budget (recent adopted budgets have run in the ~$1.7B–$1.8B range; confirm the exact FY2026 all-funds total in the county's published budget). Technology is a distinct and growing line: the IT Services department budget was reported to have risen from roughly $58M to over $70M as the county responded to its security incident. The FY2026 adopted property tax rate is $0.215500 per $100 of assessed valuation.

Initiatives & Priorities

Post-incident cybersecurity rebuild

After the October 2023 'Play' ransomware incident (data on ~200K individuals exposed), the county increased IT funding and refocused on endpoint monitoring, access controls, and resilience. Security modernization is the clearest active driver — lead with it.

Cybersecurity Resilience
IT leadership reset and governance

A permanent CIO (Justine Tran, started Nov 2024) is executing a publicly described three-phase strategic plan: Phase 1 establishes enterprise architecture, tech-stack standards, and cybersecurity; Phase 2 expands and accelerates; Phase 3 optimizes and innovates. Expect formalized IT governance, ITIL-style service management, and a re-baselining of infrastructure — an opening for refresh and standardization conversations.

Leadership Modernization
AI and data-as-a-system-of-reference

CIO Tran has publicly prioritized AI adoption ('an AI companion for every role that is possible') and turning county data into an organized, accessible 'system of reference.' Compute, storage, data-platform, and secure-AI infrastructure are natural entry points as this moves from vision to procurement.

Infrastructure and data-center modernization

A large county supporting justice, elections, health, and resident services runs aging infrastructure that the new IT leadership is positioned to refresh. Server, storage, and hybrid-cloud modernization are natural ISG entry points.

Resident-facing digital services

The county continues to expand online services (records, payments, justice and health portals). Backend compute, storage, and identity/security underpin these — connect endpoint and infrastructure plays to the citizen-services roadmap.

Procurement

Dallas County buys through its Purchasing Department, which processes thousands of purchase orders and hundreds of formal solicitations each year and posts opportunities via the BidNet platform (adopted December 2024). For technology, the county — like other Texas local governments — can buy voluntarily off DIR Cooperative Contracts and other cooperative vehicles, avoiding a full RFP. Confirm Dell's DIR and co-op coverage and register as a supplier before pursuing direct awards.
