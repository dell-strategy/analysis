Overview

The City of Houston is the government of the 4th-largest city in the United States, serving roughly 2.3 million residents under a strong-mayor structure led by Mayor John Whitmire (sworn in January 2024). Enterprise technology is centralized in Houston Information Technology Services (HITS), which delivers IT for nearly 22,000 city employees across 23 operationally diverse departments — spanning public safety, utilities, transportation, community services, financial operations, infrastructure, building-code enforcement, public health, and constituent-facing digital services. HITS frames its mission as transforming Houston into 'a digital city for all,' with emphasis on resilient infrastructure, cybersecurity, and modern digital foundations. Houston adopted a ~$7B FY2026 all-funds budget (passed June 2025) that closed a projected ~$330M shortfall through a drainage-lawsuit settlement, a voluntary-retirement incentive program (taken by roughly one-third of 2,700 eligible employees), and department consolidations — including merging the 311 call center with water billing and consolidating Houston Police Department HR and IT. For a technology seller this is a very large municipal IT buyer operating under real fiscal pressure, with stable IT leadership and a $16.7B capital improvement plan funding longer-horizon infrastructure. Houston buys through its central Strategic Procurement Division and Texas cooperative contracts, including the Texas DIR Cooperative Contracts.

Budget & Fiscal

Houston City Council passed Mayor Whitmire's ~$7B FY2026 budget in June 2025, a plan built explicitly to close a projected ~$330M General Fund shortfall without service cuts. The General Fund — the pool that funds most operating and technology services — is about $3.03B, down ~$74.5M from the prior year. A separate ~$16.7B FY2026 capital improvement plan (CIP) funds multi-year infrastructure.

Initiatives & Priorities

Digital city / e-government modernization

HITS positions itself as the catalyst to make Houston 'a digital city for all,' modernizing constituent-facing services (online permits, 311) and the underlying enterprise platforms for 23 departments. A broad lane for application modernization, end-user compute, and infrastructure refresh tied to service delivery.

Enterprise cybersecurity

HITS owns city cybersecurity for ~22,000 employees and 23 departments, including public-safety systems, under City CISO Dr. Chris Mitchell. Local government remains a top ransomware target nationally, keeping endpoint, identity, backup/recovery, and resilient-infrastructure investment on the agenda — a steady refresh-and-harden motion.

AI & emerging-tech adoption

Houston is integrating AI into municipal operations (e.g., AI-assisted permitting) and a regional Tech Policy Advisory Committee is prioritizing AI/emerging-tech policy and AI-ready digital infrastructure. Downstream needs include AI-capable compute, data platforms, and governance — and the seam between city programs and the broader Houston AI/data-center buildout.

Broadband & digital inclusion / smart city

Houston's digital agenda includes broadband access, affordability, and digital-literacy programs tied to economic mobility, alongside smart-city efforts run through the city's technology organization. A live lane for connectivity, data, and connected-infrastructure use cases.

Procurement

Houston centralizes technology buying through its Strategic Procurement Division (SPD) and leans on Texas cooperative contracts, which satisfy competitive requirements without a standalone solicitation. For a Dell seller the first moves are confirming co-op coverage and registering in the city's vendor portal.

Vehicle / gateWhat it means for Dell
City of Houston Strategic Procurement Division (SPD)The central procurement office for all city departments; vendors register and respond to solicitations via the Beacon Bid agency portal. All technology purchases — including those made through a cooperative — must be processed by SPD. The gate to any city buy.
Texas DIR Cooperative ContractsAs a Texas local government, Houston is an eligible DIR co-op customer and can buy hardware, software, and services off pre-competed statewide contracts. Confirm Dell's DIR coverage is active and correctly scoped — the fastest catalog-style path; co-op buys still route through SPD.
Other cooperative contractsHouston, like most large Texas cities, also uses other cooperative purchasing organizations for technology and catalog goods; each cooperative purchase requires a signed Request to Purchase from Cooperative/Interlocal justification. Confirm which co-ops Dell holds and which the city actively buys through for a given category.
