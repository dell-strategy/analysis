New Hampshire is a small, fiscally conservative, centralized-IT state — a high-touch, relationship-driven market rather than a big-dollar one. Governor Kelly Ayotte signed a $15.9B two-year budget (HB1/HB2) on June 27, 2025, that trimmed agencies (including a roughly $51M cut to Health & Human Services and reductions to the University System), so technology spend will favor consolidation, modernization, and security over net-new expansion. Three things shape the motion for a Dell seller. First, the Department of Information Technology (DoIT) runs a fully consolidated, agency-wide IT model and just ratified its 2025–2029 statewide IT strategy ('Digital Transformation is the Business') in December 2025. Second, cybersecurity is the dominant theme: a string of 2025 ransomware and breach incidents hit counties, courts, and school districts, and the state is pushing a 'whole-of-state' security posture under new CISO Pamela McLeod. Third, buying runs through the DAS Bureau of Purchase & Property statewide contracts, with DoIT approval required on IT purchases over $500.

Where Dell can play
  • Whole-of-state cybersecurity — endpoint, identity, backup/recovery, and resilience for state agencies plus counties, courts, and K-12 hit by 2025 attacks.
  • Infrastructure modernization and data-center consolidation under DoIT's 2025–2029 strategy (PowerEdge, storage, networking, services).
  • Client/endpoint lifecycle refresh across a fully consolidated state IT estate that DoIT manages centrally.
  • Research-computing and AI infrastructure at the University of New Hampshire (HPC, NSF/NVIDIA-funded multimodal AI work).

Dell Action Plan

  • Make DoIT the anchor account: map Dell ISG, client, and services offerings to the 2025–2029 statewide IT strategy and engage on modernization/consolidation planning, not one-off buys.
  • Lead with whole-of-state cybersecurity — endpoint, identity, immutable backup/recovery, and resilience for state plus the counties, courts, and K-12 districts hit in 2025; build the relationship with CISO Pamela McLeod's team.
  • Confirm Dell and reseller coverage on current DAS statewide contracts, and bake the DoIT >$500 IT-approval step into every deal path so it doesn't stall at close.
  • Engage the University of New Hampshire independently for HPC and AI-infrastructure (NSF/NVIDIA-funded research computing) outside the central state-agency motion.
  • Position governed, private/on-prem AI for PII-sensitive workloads consistent with DoIT's responsible AI-use policy, rather than open public-model approaches.

Budget & Fiscal

New Hampshire budgets on a two-year (biennial) cycle running July 1 to June 30. The FY2026–27 budget — HB1 (operating) and HB2 (trailer/policy) — totals roughly $15.9B and was signed by Governor Ayotte on June 27, 2025. It is a constrained budget that trimmed several agencies, so there is no large standalone 'state IT budget' line; technology spend is centralized under DoIT and distributed across agency operating budgets, with security and modernization prioritized over expansion.

Whole-of-State Cybersecurity7

Active priority

2025 brought a wave of incidents — Hillsborough County systems offline, Strafford County court disruptions, and ransomware/breaches at Pelham/Hudson school districts and Coös County health services. Broadband Expansion (BEAD / BIL)4

In execution

In effect

DoIT maintains a statewide policy on AI and Automated Decision Systems setting principles for responsible, ethical use across agencies, with strong emphasis on protecting Personal Identifying Information (PII). NH is taking a measured, governance-first approach rather than a funded AI program — an opening to lead with governed, private/on-prem AI for sensitive state data rather than open public models.

Procurement & Competition

Procurement is centralized. The Department of Administrative Services (DAS) Bureau of Purchase & Property runs most state goods and non-construction services through statewide contracts, and most buys post there rather than agency-direct. The Department of Information Technology (DoIT) is the central technology authority: it coordinates, reviews, and approves statewide IT activities, and any IT equipment purchase over $500 requires DoIT review and a DoIT approval number.

Competitive landscape

New Hampshire is a small, centralized, relationship-driven market where price discipline and statewide-contract presence matter more than scale. Because DoIT consolidates IT and gates purchases, the winning motion is fewer, deeper relationships — aligning to the 2025–2029 strategy and the whole-of-state cyber agenda — rather than chasing many independent agency deals.
