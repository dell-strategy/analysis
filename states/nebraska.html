Nebraska's technology story for a Dell seller is defined by two forces pulling in opposite directions. On one side, the state is one of the hottest hyperscale data-center markets in the country: Google has invested more than $3.5B since 2019 across Papillion, Omaha and Lincoln, Meta's Sarpy County campus is expanding toward a nine-building, ~4M sq ft footprint, and a Google-linked proposal in southeast Nebraska could draw 1,000–3,000 MW — more than three times the city of Lincoln. On the other side, the state faces a roughly $471M budget deficit, and Governor Pillen has directed cabinet agencies to cut hundreds of millions, so State CIO Matthew McCarville is running the first major OCIO reorganization in 20 years explicitly to reduce IT cost. The result is a buyer that is modernizing aggressively (mainframe migration, AI/data Center of Excellence in Omaha, a Joint Cybersecurity Operations Command Center) but scrutinizing every dollar. Buying runs centrally through OCIO with statewide contracts via DAS Materiel; the AI-infrastructure tailwind from the hyperscaler buildout is the strongest Dell angle.

Where Dell can play
  • AI-infrastructure / 'AI factory' pull from the Google and Meta hyperscale buildout in Sarpy County and across the state (PowerEdge, networking, services, power-and-cooling adjacency).
  • OCIO modernization: mainframe migration off 1960s-era systems and consolidation of duplicative hardware/applications across agencies — a refresh and platform play.
  • Joint Cybersecurity Operations Command Center serving state, local, and education — secure compute and infrastructure for a whole-of-state SOC.
  • Data & AI Center of Excellence in Omaha — governed, on-prem/private AI on clean state data, aligned to a cost-conscious, data-governance-first CIO.

Dell Action Plan

  • Lead with AI-infrastructure: tie Dell ISG / AI Factory offerings to the Google and Meta hyperscale buildout and to University of Nebraska research-computing buyers.
  • Frame state-government modernization as cost reduction — map PowerEdge refresh, mainframe migration, and consolidation to OCIO's $13M overhead-cut goal and the deficit mandate.
  • Engage the Joint Cybersecurity Operations Command Center as a whole-of-state SOC opportunity — secure compute, infrastructure, and managed services across state/local/education.
  • Position governed, private/on-prem AI on clean state data with the Omaha Data & AI Center of Excellence and the chief data officer, aligned to LB 525-era transparency expectations.

Budget & Fiscal

Nebraska budgets on a two-year (biennial) cycle. The 2025–27 biennium mainline budget is roughly $11B per year, set against an estimated ~$471M deficit that has pushed Governor Pillen to direct cabinet agencies toward large recurring cuts. There is no single published 'state IT budget' line — technology spend is distributed across agencies and the centrally-run Office of the CIO (OCIO), which is deliberately lowering its internal rates to cut cost for the agencies it serves.

Procurement & Competition

Nebraska runs technology centrally through the Office of the CIO (OCIO), with statewide purchasing administered by the DAS Materiel Division. Agencies are generally required to use contracts the Materiel Division designates for statewide use unless the materiel administrator permits otherwise. Active state contracts are publicly searchable, and the Nebraska Information Technology Commission (NITC) sets statewide IT policy and standards.

Competitive landscape

Nebraska is a centralized, cost-disciplined market. With a sizable share of IT staff retirement-eligible and a budget deficit forcing cuts, differentiation comes from total-cost-of-ownership, modernization-as-savings narratives, AI-infrastructure capability tied to the hyperscaler buildout, and services that map to OCIO's consolidation and cyber-SOC priorities — not price alone.
