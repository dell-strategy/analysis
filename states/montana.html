Montana is a small-population, biennially-budgeted state that has become unexpectedly important in technology policy. Governor Greg Gianforte — a software entrepreneur (RightNow Technologies) before politics — signed the nation's first 'Right to Compute Act' (SB 212) in April 2025, codifying a light-touch, pro-compute stance rather than restrictive AI regulation. The 2025 Legislature (69th) enacted the ~$16.6B 2027-biennium budget (HB 2) plus the Long-Range IT Program (HB 10, ~$54M) that funds SITSD modernization, mainframe retirement, and cybersecurity. On November 18, 2025 NTIA approved Montana's final BEAD proposal — roughly $629M to close rural broadband gaps under ConnectMT. And a gigawatt-scale data-center rush (Project Cardinal in Great Falls, Atlas/Sabey in Butte, Quantica's 'Big Sky Campus') is testing the grid and creating private AI-infrastructure demand. For Dell, the buying motion runs through SITSD and the State Procurement Services Division's term contracts; the opportunity is modernization, whole-of-state cybersecurity, and AI-ready infrastructure for both government and the incoming data-center operators.

Where Dell can play
  • SITSD modernization under HB 10 (LRITP ~$54M) — mainframe/legacy retirement, server/storage refresh, and cloud migration for a centralized state IT shop.
  • Whole-of-state cybersecurity — extend the University of Montana SOC model (5,000+ endpoints across counties and towns) with secure compute, endpoint, and managed services.
  • Private/on-prem AI aligned to the state's 'Right to Compute' posture — governed AI on sensitive workloads without heavy regulatory friction.
  • AI-infrastructure demand from the data-center boom (Great Falls, Butte, Broadview) — PowerEdge, networking, and services for new operators landing in-state.

Budget & Fiscal

Montana budgets on a two-year (biennial) cycle. The 69th Legislature's HB 2 appropriates roughly $16.6B all-funds for the 2027 biennium (signed by Gov. Gianforte in June 2025) — about a 2% increase over the prior biennium, with federal dollars making up nearly half. There is no single 'state IT budget'; technology spend is centralized largely through SITSD's billable-service model (200+ services) and funded for major projects through the separate Long-Range Information Technology Program (HB 10).

Initiatives & Policy

Right to Compute Act (SB 212)6

In effect

Signed April 2025, Montana became the first state to codify a 'right to privately own and use computational resources for lawful purposes,' subjecting any restriction to strict scrutiny. For critical-infrastructure AI, deployers must adopt a NIST-aligned risk-management policy. The pro-compute, low-regulation posture favors private/on-prem AI adoption — a chance for Dell to lead with governed, auditable AI rather than wait on heavy compliance gates.

ConnectMT / BEAD Broadband Buildout4

Funded — deploying

NTIA approved Montana's final BEAD proposal in November 2025, awarding roughly $629M to bring service to unserved/underserved rural locations; subgrantees have four years to build. This follows a $270M ARPA round (61 projects, 2022). Dollars flow to ISPs, but the buildout strengthens SITSD's statewide network role and the digital-government surface Dell can support.

Whole-of-State Cybersecurity7

Maturing

Montana pairs SITSD's Network Operations Security Center with a University of Montana Security Operations Center (HB 10-funded) that monitors 5,000+ endpoints across counties (Sheridan, Ravalli, Lake) and towns (Whitefish, Manhattan, Harlowton), plus a Cyber Range and rural policy academy with CISA and the National Guard. Extending endpoint, secure-compute, and managed services to thin-staffed local governments is a strong Dell fit.

Procurement & Competition

Technology procurement in Montana is centralized under the Department of Administration. The State Information Technology Services Division (SITSD) owns shared IT infrastructure and services, while the State Procurement Services Division (SPSD / State Procurement Bureau) runs competitive solicitations and establishes statewide term contracts that state agencies — and, where indicated, cooperative purchasers — buy from at predetermined prices.

Competitive landscape

Montana is a small, centralized, relationship-driven market — fewer seats than a large state, but decisions concentrate in SITSD and SPSD, so coverage on the right term contracts matters more than volume of accounts. Differentiation comes from modernization/lifecycle value, whole-of-state cybersecurity for thin-staffed locals, and AI-ready infrastructure that fits the state's pro-compute posture — not price alone.
