Overview

Minnesota IT Services (MNIT) is the state's consolidated IT agency, created by Minnesota's 2011 IT consolidation law to centralize technology across the executive branch. Led by the Commissioner / State Chief Information Officer, MNIT builds, maintains, and secures the state's infrastructure, applications, networks, and cybersecurity for more than 70 agencies, boards, and commissions, and employs roughly 2,800 people. It also operates MNET, the statewide network that connects all 87 counties, ~300 cities, and the state's public higher-education campuses. MNIT is best understood as a shared-services provider: most of its funding comes from charging partner agencies for the services it delivers (an internal service / enterprise technology fund) rather than a large general-fund appropriation — only a small share (~5%) is direct general-fund money. For a Dell SLG seller, MNIT is both a large enterprise IT buyer in its own right and the central authority that shapes standards, security gates, and shared infrastructure that agency-level deals must align to.

Budget & Fiscal

MNIT runs primarily on a chargeback model: it bills partner agencies for the IT services it delivers through its enterprise technology / internal service fund, with only about 5% of funding from the general fund. Reported total expenses across all customers were roughly $843M in FY2024, and the FY2026-27 budgeted cost base rose about 14% over FY2024-25, driven by salary/fringe carry-forward and software volumes (including a Microsoft G5 desktop license upgrade).

  • Because MNIT is chargeback-funded, much of the real buying power sits in the partner-agency budgets it bills — the agency is the delivery and standards layer as much as the wallet.
  • MNIT planned to acquire capital equipment via Master Lease (reported ~$15M in FY26 and ~$13M in FY27) for SAN, LAN/WAN, server, and desktop/laptop refresh — a recurring infrastructure-refresh signal.
  • MNIT has carried a cash-assistance loan from Minnesota Management & Budget (reported at ~$60M, repayable end of biennium) and signaled intent to request a renewed loan — a sign of the cash-flow pressure inherent in a chargeback model.

Initiatives & Priorities

Secure generative AI for state employees

MNIT has stood up secure GenAI capability for the executive branch — including support for Microsoft 365 Copilot Chat in the government environment (rolled out from April 2025) and pilots of a secure AI platform (nebulaONE) for RAG, custom chatbots, and agents. No-cost responsible-AI training (via InnovateUS) has reached 1,000+ state employees. AI governance is anchored in MNIT's public AI security standard. Position infrastructure, GPU/compute, and data-platform capabilities behind responsible-AI adoption.

Whole-of-State cybersecurity

MNIT, with the Minnesota Cybersecurity Task Force, runs the Whole-of-State Cybersecurity Plan distributing federal SLCGP plus state funding (initially ~$23.5M) to local governments, Tribal Nations, and schools, with most funding passed through to locals. MNIT's Security Operations Center and Threat & Vulnerability Management Unit deliver subsidized shared services. A statewide security buying center that reaches beyond state agencies into county/city/K-12.

LoginMN statewide identity & access management

MNIT is rolling out LoginMN, a login.gov-inspired constituent identity, identity-proofing, and MFA platform governed by a new Constituent Identity and Access Management Standard. Legislatively funded and tied to the state's anti-fraud push, with a reported July 1, 2027 mandate for all state digital services that collect the public's private data to move onto the platform. A statewide identity and fraud-prevention layer that Dell security and infrastructure conversations should align to.

Procurement

Selling to MNIT and other Minnesota public entities runs largely through the state's Office of State Procurement (within the Department of Administration) and cooperative master contracts — confirm Dell's coverage on the relevant state and NASPO ValuePoint vehicles before pursuing deals. MNIT centralizes much enterprise buying, but agency-level purchases still flow through these statewide vehicles.

Vehicle / gateWhat it means for Dell
Office of State Procurement (OSP) master contractsMinnesota's central procurement office (Dept. of Administration) establishes statewide master contracts that executive-branch agencies and many local entities buy from — the primary path for IT hardware, software, and services.
NASPO ValuePoint cooperative contractsMinnesota participates in NASPO ValuePoint (the former WSCA-NASPO) cooperative portfolio, including Computer Equipment and Cloud Solutions categories. Buying off these requires a Minnesota Participating Addendum — confirm Dell's PA coverage.
MNIT chargeback / enterprise servicesFor consolidated executive-branch services, MNIT itself is the buyer and bills partner agencies — infrastructure, security, and managed services can route through MNIT's enterprise delivery rather than an individual agency RFP.
