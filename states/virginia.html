Virginia is the single most important state in the country for AI infrastructure: Loudoun County's 'Data Center Alley' is the densest concentration of data centers on the planet, and the hyperscale buildout shapes the state's politics, power grid, and tax base. For an SLG seller, three things matter most. First, that data-center boom is both an economic engine and a political fight — 15 data-center bills passed the 2026 General Assembly and a ~$1.6B/year sales-and-use tax exemption is the central dispute that has stalled the 2026–28 budget into June 2026. Second, government technology buying is unusually centralized: VITA (the Virginia IT Agency) sets policy, runs statewide contracts, and gates cloud through its COV Ramp security review. Third, leadership just turned over — Gov. Spanberger took office in January 2026 and named Mike Watson Commonwealth CIO in April 2026, with a stated focus on cloud transformation and AI adoption. Dell's strongest plays are AI-infrastructure / 'AI factory' demand and a centralized, governed buying motion through VITA.

Where Dell can play
  • AI-infrastructure and 'AI factory' demand tied to the world's largest data-center market and the surrounding ecosystem (PowerEdge, networking, services).
  • Enterprise cloud transformation and AI adoption under a new Commonwealth CIO — modernization across 60-plus executive-branch agencies.
  • Governed, auditable AI to support the state's first-in-the-nation agentic-AI regulatory-review pilot and emerging AI-in-schools guidance.
  • Endpoint, client, and lifecycle refresh across a large centralized executive-branch IT footprint managed through VITA.

Budget & Fiscal

Virginia budgets on a two-year (biennial) cycle running July 1 to June 30, with the General Assembly amending the off-year budget annually. After a months-long impasse (regular session ended March 14 without a deal; an April 23 special session failed), negotiators reached a ~$74B compromise on June 20, 2026, with floor votes scheduled June 23 and signing required before the July 1 deadline — the data-center sales/use tax exemption is retained but paired with a new $0.011/kWh data-center energy tax. As of June 22 the budget is agreed but not yet voted or signed. There is no single 'state IT budget' line — technology spend is distributed across agencies and run through VITA.

Initiatives & Policy

Data Center Alley & AI-Infrastructure Buildout2

Dominant / contested

Loudoun County hosts the world's densest concentration of data centers, generating roughly 38% of the county's general-fund revenue. The 2026 General Assembly passed 15 of 61 data-center bills — adding siting/site-assessment requirements for 100MW+ facilities (HB 153), shifting grid-capacity costs to 25MW+ high-load users (HB 1393, HB 284), and water-reporting rules. The buildout is the strongest tailwind in the country for AI-infrastructure and ISG demand.

Agentic-AI Regulatory Review Pilot (EO 51)6

Live

Executive Order 51 (July 2025) made Virginia the first state to deploy an agentic-AI tool to scan regulations and guidance for redundancy and statutory conflicts; from end-2025, executive-branch agencies must fold AI into their periodic regulatory reviews. Most broader AI legislation was tabled to 2027, so the near-term motion is operational AI adoption rather than new mandates — a chance to lead with governed, auditable AI.

COV Ramp Cloud Security Review7

Mandatory gate

COV Ramp (formerly ECOS), run by VITA, evaluates the security programs of SaaS/PaaS suppliers before agencies may procure them; 400-plus cloud products are assessed and approvals are valid 12 months and must be renewed. Any Dell cloud/as-a-service offering touching Commonwealth data must clear COV Ramp and align to the SEC530 security standard — a gate to plan for early.

Procurement & Competition

The Virginia IT Agency (VITA) is the centralized technology authority for the executive branch — it sets IT policy and security standards, runs statewide contracts, and manages sourcing for more than 60 agencies and 65,000-plus users. Most state technology buys run through VITA statewide contracts and its multisupplier service model, with delegated agency procurements still subject to VITA oversight.

Competitive landscape

Virginia is a centralized, contract-driven market — incumbents hold VITA statewide agreements across compute, networking, and services, and the dense federal/contractor presence in Northern Virginia makes it a sophisticated buyer. Differentiation comes from COV Ramp-ready offerings, AI-infrastructure capability tied to the data-center ecosystem, and services that map to cloud/AI modernization rather than price alone.
