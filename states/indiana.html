Braun budget discipline / I-DOGE

Indiana enters the FY2026-27 biennium under a cost-cutting Braun administration that paired a flat ~$46.2B General Fund budget with a statewide 5% spending reduction and an 'I-DOGE' efficiency review. For a Dell seller, three things matter. First, new State CIO Warren Lenard (appointed March 2025) carries an explicit mandate to consolidate IT across state agencies for better service at lower cost — a refresh-and-rationalize motion that favors standardized, well-priced infrastructure and devices. Second, Indiana is now one of the country's hottest AI-infrastructure markets: Meta broke ground on a $10B, 1GW data-center campus at the LEAP district in Lebanon (Feb 2026), SK hynix is building a $3.87B HBM advanced-packaging plant at Purdue Research Park, and Amazon, Google and Microsoft projects are landing across the state — all backed by aggressive data-center sales-tax exemptions. Third, the state's buying motion runs through IDOA Quantity Purchase Agreements (QPAs), so Dell's coverage and reseller alignment there is the gate to agency deals.

Where Dell can play
  • AI-infrastructure / 'AI factory' demand tied to the LEAP Lebanon and statewide data-center boom plus Purdue/SK hynix research compute (PowerEdge, networking, services).
  • Statewide IT consolidation under CIO Lenard — standardized compute, endpoints, and lifecycle services delivered through IOT at lower cost.
  • University and research-computing buyers (Purdue, IU) scaling AI and semiconductor R&D.
  • Governed AI adoption under the state's NIST-aligned AI policy and AI Task Force — private/on-prem AI for sensitive agency workloads.

Dell Action Plan

  • Lead the AI-infrastructure conversation: tie Dell ISG / AI Factory offerings to the LEAP Lebanon buildout, SK hynix/Purdue ecosystem, and university research-computing buyers.
  • Validate Dell's IDOA QPA coverage and authorized resellers are current and correctly scoped for the agencies you're targeting.
  • Engage CIO Warren Lenard's IOT consolidation agenda with standardized compute, endpoints, and lifecycle services that lower per-unit and operating cost.
  • Frame proposals around Braun-era cost discipline (I-DOGE, 5% cuts) — quantify TCO and consolidation savings, not just unit price.
  • Position governed, private/on-prem AI for sensitive workloads as agencies operationalize the state's NIST-aligned AI policy and Task Force guidance.

Budget & Fiscal

Indiana budgets on a two-year (biennial) cycle. The 2025 General Assembly's HEA 1001 funds FY2026-27 with roughly $23.0B in FY2026 and $23.2B in FY2027 of General Fund appropriations (~$46.2B total), including about $604M in capital. Facing a softer April 2025 revenue forecast, the budget applies a statewide ~5% reduction on most line items plus an additional 5% operating reserve. There is no single 'state IT budget' line — technology spend is distributed across agencies and recovered through the Indiana Office of Technology's internal-service rate model.

Procurement & Competition

The Indiana Department of Administration (IDOA) Procurement Division is the central purchasing authority, while the Indiana Office of Technology (IOT) sets technology standards and delivers shared infrastructure. Most state agency technology buys run through IDOA Quantity Purchase Agreements (QPAs) — pre-competed statewide contracts that let agencies buy from qualified vendors without a separate solicitation, with typical PO processing of just a few days. Many IT QPAs ride established GSA/cooperative instruments.

Competitive landscape

Indiana is a cost-conscious, contract-driven market — incumbents hold QPAs across compute, networking and services, and the Braun administration's efficiency focus puts pressure on price. Differentiation comes from AI-infrastructure capability tied to the data-center buildout, consolidation-friendly standardization, and services that map to IOT's modernization rather than price alone.
