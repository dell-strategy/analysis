Overview

Suffolk County is one of New York State's largest counties — roughly 1.5 million residents across eastern Long Island — and a major local-government technology buyer in its own right. Central IT is delivered by the county Department of Information Technology (DoIT), led by a CIO / IT Commissioner appointed by the County Executive. The account is best understood through the lens of the September 2022 ransomware attack: the AlphV/BlackCat intrusion (traced to a Log4j exploit in the County Clerk's office in 2021) crippled police dispatch, took the county's main website offline for months, forced more than 10,000 workers off email and onto pen-and-paper, and exposed personal data on hundreds of thousands of residents and employees. A special legislative investigation found there had been no countywide CISO, no incident-response plan, and fragmented IT teams. The fallout reset the county's technology posture: under County Executive Ed Romaine (took office January 2024) the county has hired a CIO/DoIT Commissioner, funded an internal CISO plus a 24/7 virtual CISO, and pursued infrastructure modernization and cloud migration. For a Dell SLG seller, Suffolk is a cyber-resilience-and-modernization account where security, infrastructure refresh, and recovery readiness are the durable demand drivers.

Budget & Fiscal

Suffolk County is a direct spender, not a procurement conduit — its leverage is its own operating budget and capital program. County Executive Romaine presented a ~$4.3B recommended 2026 operating budget; IT and cybersecurity sit inside that as a deliberately funded priority following the 2022 attack, including funding for an internal CISO and a virtual CISO. The fully loaded cost of the ransomware recovery has been documented at roughly $25.7M, well above early estimates.

Initiatives & Priorities

Post-ransomware cyber rebuild & resilience

The central program: standing up countywide cybersecurity governance the 2022 attack found missing — an internal CISO plus a 24/7 virtual CISO for redundancy, incident-response capability, and a hardened security posture across DoIT-served departments. Security, recovery readiness, and resilience are the durable demand drivers for this account.

Cybersecurity Resilience
Financial/ERP system modernization to cloud (CGI Advantage)

The county upgraded and migrated its CGI Advantage ERP financial management system to CGI Advantage Cloud (completed around April 2026), partnering with the Comptroller and County Executive's office. The county plans to integrate additional financial and administrative functions on the cloud foundation over time — an ongoing modernization track.

Infrastructure modernization & cyber resilience

Beyond the financial system, the county has committed to modernizing technology infrastructure and bolstering cyber resilience — infrastructure refresh, secure architecture, and transitioning core systems toward modern, resilient environments. A natural fit for infrastructure, security, and recovery-readiness conversations.

Infrastructure Modernization
DoIT governance & CISO function maturation

The legislative post-mortem flagged fragmented IT teams and no central security leadership. Maturing DoIT governance — a named CIO/Commissioner, a standing CISO function, and consolidated security operations — is an ongoing organizational effort that shapes how and through whom technology decisions get made.

Procurement

Selling to Suffolk County runs through its centralized purchasing plus the cooperative vehicles New York local governments are allowed to use. The county's Office of Central Procurement administers commodity buys, and the county explicitly recognizes piggybacking on established cooperative contracts — most importantly NYS OGS centralized contracts — so confirm Dell's coverage on those vehicles first.
