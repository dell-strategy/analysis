Overview

The New York City Department of Health and Mental Hygiene (DOHMH, branded "NYC Health") is one of the largest and oldest municipal public-health agencies in the United States, with roots going back to 1805. It serves roughly 8.3 million residents and runs an exceptionally broad mission: communicable- and chronic-disease surveillance and outbreak response, immunizations, the City's public-health laboratory, vital records (birth/death certificates), restaurant and environmental-health inspections, school health, and mental-hygiene (behavioral-health) services. DOHMH is fundamentally data- and surveillance-intensive — its Bureau of Communicable Disease tracks 70+ reportable conditions, and it operates citywide systems for syndromic surveillance, immunization registries, electronic lab reporting, and population-health analytics. Importantly, DOHMH does not run citywide IT on its own: like all NYC agencies it sits under the Office of Technology and Innovation (OTI) for shared infrastructure, citywide contracts, cloud review, and cybersecurity (NYC Cyber Command), while maintaining its own Division of Information Technology and informatics teams for mission-specific health systems. A January 2026 mayoral transition installed a new administration and a new Health Commissioner, so leadership and priorities are still settling.

Budget & Fiscal

DOHMH is a large direct spender on a citywide health mission, but it is not a central IT buyer — its technology dollars flow through OTI's citywide vehicles and its own mission-IT division rather than a standalone procurement authority. The FY2026 Executive Budget proposed roughly $2.31 billion for DOHMH, split across a Public Health division (~$1.42B) and a Mental Hygiene division (~$775M). At Commissioner Martin's January 2026 appointment, the agency was described as having more than 7,000 employees and an annual budget of about $1.6 billion (a figure that reflects City-funded operations and excludes large pass-through/grant streams).

Initiatives & Priorities

Data Modernization Initiative & Center for Population Health Data Science

DOHMH stood up a Center for Population Health Data Science (launched Oct 2023) to drive its Data Modernization Initiative — building modern data infrastructure that integrates legacy systems, links public-health, healthcare, and social-service data, and enables responsible AI and advanced analytics. This is the agency's flagship data/AI workstream and the natural anchor for compute, storage, and data-platform conversations.

Disease surveillance & outbreak-response systems

The Bureau of Communicable Disease tracks 70+ reportable conditions and runs citywide syndromic surveillance, electronic lab reporting, and registries — systems stressed by recent measles, mpox, and H5N1 avian-influenza vigilance. Sustained demand for resilient, scalable infrastructure and faster data ingestion/analytics.

Surveillance Resilience
Public Health Laboratory & informatics

DOHMH operates the City's public-health laboratory, a high-throughput environment generating large volumes of test, genomic-sequencing, and pathogen data that feed surveillance. Lab informatics, data integration, and high-performance compute/storage are ongoing modernization needs.

Responsible AI & data governance

AI/advanced-analytics adoption is governed within DOHMH's data-modernization program and under NYC's citywide AI Action Plan and OTI governance. Any AI offering sold into the agency must clear responsible-AI and data-privacy gates given the sensitivity of health data.

Procurement

Selling into DOHMH means navigating two layers: NYC's citywide contracting machinery and OTI's role as the technology gatekeeper. DOHMH buys through its own Agency Chief Contracting Officer (ACCO) under the Mayor's Office of Contract Services (MOCS), but technology — especially cloud and shared infrastructure — flows through OTI's citywide/requirement contracts and must clear OTI's cloud-review and Cyber Command security gates. Confirm Dell's position on citywide and DCAS vehicles before forecasting.
