Overview

The Georgia Technology Authority (GTA) is the state's central IT agency. It sets statewide technology policy and security standards, must endorse/approve technology Georgia agencies use, and delivers shared infrastructure through the Georgia Enterprise Technology Services (GETS) program — a long-running public-private managed-services model rather than a state-run data center. Under GETS, GTA coordinates a multi-vendor ecosystem (with Capgemini serving as the multi-sourcing service integrator and providers such as AT&T, NTT DATA, Unisys, Atos, and Xerox delivering network, end-user, server, mainframe, and print services) to supply IT to a core set of executive-branch agencies, while managed network services reach more than 100,000 users across 1,200-plus state and local locations. GTA also houses the state Office of Information Security (led by the State CISO) and an Office of Artificial Intelligence created in 2023, making it the policy gate for security, cloud, and AI across Georgia government. For a Dell seller, GTA is best understood as the policy authority and integration hub — much agency IT is bought and delivered through GETS contracts and statewide vehicles, with day-to-day purchasing also touching the Department of Administrative Services (DOAS) State Purchasing Division.

Budget & Fiscal

GTA's leverage is structural, not a large direct appropriation: it sets policy, must endorse agency technology buys, and routes infrastructure through the GETS managed-services contracts rather than spending a big general-revenue IT line itself. Public, current dollar figures for total GETS contract value are not consistently published; the most citable scale metrics are operational (users and sites served) plus the state's published GETS Ready convenience-contract program.

Initiatives & Priorities

Office of Artificial Intelligence & AI governance

GTA stood up an Office of Artificial Intelligence in 2023 with an AI Advisory Council and guiding principles, and in 2025 issued a Generative AI Responsible Use Standard (SS-25-001) requiring human review, labeling, prompt/output recordkeeping, annual audits, and 48-hour incident reporting. State CIO Shawnzia Thomas frames 2026 as 'AI governance is security' — every AI buy must clear the same security bar.

AI Governance Security
GETS managed-services model

GTA coordinates the multi-vendor GETS ecosystem (Capgemini as service integrator; AT&T, NTT DATA, Unisys, Atos, Xerox as service providers) delivering infrastructure, end-user, server, mainframe, network, and print services. Infrastructure refresh and modernization run through this model, not direct agency procurement.

Managed Services Infrastructure Modernization
Cybersecurity & Office of Information Security

The State CISO leads GTA's Office of Information Security, unifying security across the enterprise, GETS, and GTA. The state runs large-scale cyber exercises (e.g., Cyber Dawg) and applies uniform controls — access management, audit logging, data protection — across both traditional and AI systems.

Cybersecurity Compliance
Innovation Lab & responsible experimentation

GTA opened an Innovation Lab in July 2025 to test emerging technologies using synthetic rather than production data, paired with workforce upskilling partnerships (Google, Microsoft, AWS, Palo Alto, Oracle). A useful entry point for AI-infrastructure and proof-of-concept positioning.

Procurement

Selling to Georgia state government runs through several layers: GTA must endorse/approve technology agencies use and delivers infrastructure via GETS, while routine commodity IT buys flow through DOAS State Purchasing Division statewide contracts. Confirm which path a given opportunity uses before forecasting — managed-services delivery (GETS) and product/reseller buys (statewide contracts) are different motions.
