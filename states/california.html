California is the largest single state-government technology market in the U.S., and FY2025-26 brings three shifts every SLG seller should track. First, the state is operating under fiscal pressure: the Budget Act of 2025 ($321.1B total, ~$228.4B General Fund) closed an ~$11.8B shortfall with cuts and reserve draws, so deals must map to mandated modernization, not discretionary spend. Second, leadership turned over — long-time CIO Liana Bailey-Crimmins retired in spring 2026 and Governor Newsom appointed Chris Given (ex-IRS Direct File, GovOps) as the new CDT Director and State CIO, with legacy-system modernization, digital identity, and AI as stated priorities. Third, AI governance is now central: Executive Order N-5-26 (March 2026) creates an AI vendor-certification and procurement framework, while SB 53 — the nation's first frontier-AI transparency law — took effect January 1, 2026. For Dell, the buying motion runs through DGS / CMAS and agency contracts; AI-infrastructure, endpoint refresh, and the Cal-Secure security buildout are the strongest entry points.

Where Dell can play
  • AI-infrastructure demand as agencies operationalize GenAI pilots (Caltrans, CDTFA, Microsoft 365 Copilot) under new governance — an opening for governed private/on-prem AI (PowerEdge, networking, services).
  • Cal-Secure five-year security roadmap modernization across CDT/OIS and state entities — secure compute, endpoint, and managed services.
  • Legacy-system and benefits modernization (EDDNext and similar) under a new CIO focused on retiring technical debt — server, storage, and services refresh.
  • Endpoint, client, and lifecycle refresh across the nation's largest state workforce, plus UC/CSU/community-college research and campus buyers.

Dell Action Plan

  • Lead with governed, private/on-prem AI for sensitive workloads as agencies operationalize GenAI under EO N-5-26 and SB 53 — position Dell AI Factory / ISG as the auditable alternative to public models.
  • Engage the new CIO (Chris Given) and CDT/GovOps early around legacy-system and benefits modernization (EDDNext-style) — server, storage, and services refresh.
  • Map offerings to the Cal-Secure roadmap with CDT's Office of Information Security — secure compute, endpoint, and managed services.
  • Validate Dell's CMAS coverage and reseller scope are current and correctly mapped to target state, local, and UC/CSU buyers before pursuing deals.
  • Frame all proposals to a constrained FY2025-26 budget — anchor to mandated modernization, federal broadband/BEAD adjacency, and measurable efficiency gains rather than discretionary spend.

Budget & Fiscal

California budgets on an annual cycle (fiscal year July 1-June 30). The Budget Act of 2025, signed June 27, 2025, appropriates roughly $321.1B in total spending with about $228.4B from the General Fund. The plan closed an estimated $11.8B shortfall through ~$2.8B in cuts, reserve draws, and fund shifts, so FY2025-26 is a constrained environment. There is no single 'state IT budget' line — technology spend is distributed across agencies plus the California Department of Technology (CDT), whose own expenditures run roughly $797M (largely the Technology Services Revolving Fund).

Initiatives & Policy

EO N-5-26 — AI Procurement & Vendor Certification5

Signed Mar 2026

Executive Order N-5-26 (March 30, 2026) directs DGS and CDT to build a vendor-certification and procurement framework for AI: vendors must attest to safeguards against illegal content, harmful bias, and civil-rights harms, and the state CISO may challenge federal supply-chain risk designations. Recommendations are due ~120 days out. Agencies will need governed, auditable AI — an opening for private/on-prem AI on sensitive workloads.

Scaling

California has moved GenAI from pilot to production: Caltrans traffic/safety analytics, a CDTFA call-center assistant, a State Digital Assistant tool across eight departments, and Microsoft 365 Copilot Chat offered at no cost to departments. EO N-5-26 pushes vetted GenAI tools to employees with safeguards. Production AI workloads drive infrastructure, networking, and services demand — and a governed private-AI alternative for sensitive data.

Cal-Secure Cybersecurity Roadmap7

In execution

Cal-Secure is California's multi-year information-security maturity roadmap, led by CDT's Office of Information Security with Cal-CSIC, Cal OES, CHP, and the Military Department. It spans people, process, and technology priorities and drives sustained investment in security tooling and services across state entities — secure compute, endpoint, and managed-services opportunity.

SB 53 — Frontier AI Transparency Act6

In effect Jan 1, 2026

SB 53 (signed Sept 2025, effective Jan 1, 2026) is the first U.S. law directly regulating frontier-AI developers, requiring published safety frameworks, transparency reports, catastrophic-risk disclosures, and whistleblower protections. It targets large model developers rather than buyers, but it sets the policy backdrop California agencies operate in and reinforces demand for transparent, governed AI approaches.

Broadband: Last Mile + Middle Mile + BEAD9

Deploying

California is deploying broadband at historic scale: the CPUC's Last Mile Federal Funding Account awarded ~$1.1B across 113 projects, with further grants in late 2025/early 2026 and ~$550M slated for FY2027-28. The state holds an ~$1.8B BEAD allocation (more eligible locations than any other state) plus a statewide middle-mile network — adjacent edge, networking, and compute infrastructure demand.

Procurement & Competition

The California Department of General Services (DGS) is the central procurement authority; the California Department of Technology (CDT) sets IT policy, oversight, and statewide services. Most state and local technology buys run through DGS leveraged procurement vehicles — chiefly the California Multiple Award Schedules (CMAS), which extend federal GSA pricing into California so agencies can buy from pre-approved vendors without a full competitive bid.

Competitive landscape

California is a large, mature, contract-driven market — incumbents hold CMAS and DGS agreements across compute, networking, and services, and the state's procurement and AI-governance rules add compliance overhead. Differentiation comes from AI-infrastructure capability, governed/private-AI for sensitive data, Cal-Secure-aligned security, and services that map to legacy modernization rather than price alone.
