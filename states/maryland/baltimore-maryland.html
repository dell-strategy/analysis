Overview

The City of Baltimore is Maryland's largest municipality, and its technology function is consolidated in the Baltimore City Office of Information & Technology (BCIT), which builds and operates the systems city agencies rely on and runs the city's digital-equity programs (skills training, free public wifi, device and connectivity access). Baltimore's IT identity is shaped by the May 2019 RobbinHood ransomware attack, which crippled most city systems for months and ran an estimated $18M+ in recovery and lost revenue; since then the city has steadily rebuilt its security posture, hired a permanent CISO, and invested in modernization. In March 2026 Mayor Brandon Scott appointed T.J. Mayotte — a 20-year technology and cybersecurity executive and former Howard County CIO — as the city's new CIO, signaling continued emphasis on cyber, service modernization, and emerging technology. For a Dell SLG seller, Baltimore is a mid-to-large municipal account where security-hardening, infrastructure refresh, endpoint/client, and digital-equity device programs are the natural entry points.

Budget & Fiscal

Baltimore's total operating budget for FY2027 (effective July 1, 2026) is roughly $4.9B — a ~7.6% increase over FY2026 — passed by the City Council 14-0 and signed by Mayor Scott on June 25, 2026. The FY2027 budget carries several IT-relevant lines: ~$12.4M to replace the emergency Computer-Aided Dispatch (CAD) system, added IT-infrastructure investment framed around reducing contractor reliance by building internal capacity, broadband/public-wifi expansion to public-housing residents, and a relaunched Innovation Fund. IT is funded within this envelope and the broader 10-year financial plan.
