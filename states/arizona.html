Arizona is one of the most important AI-infrastructure stories in the country, and the buying motion is driven more by economic-development gravity than by a single big appropriation. TSMC's Phoenix campus has scaled from $12B to a committed ~$165B (six fabs, advanced packaging, R&D), and the company bought another 900 acres at a January 2026 state land auction — the largest foreign greenfield investment in U.S. history. Around it, Arizona has become a top-two U.S. data-center market alongside Texas, with multi-gigawatt projects (Hassayampa Ranch, the La Osa/Vermaland park) landing fast. On the state side, Governor Hobbs signed an $18.3B bipartisan FY2027 budget on June 13, 2026 (the fiscal year begins July 1, 2026) that includes a three-year moratorium on data-center tax incentives and ~$1.4B in tax cuts — a signal the state is managing growth, not stopping it. Her 'Arizona Capacity and Efficiency' (ACE) Initiative consolidates IT purchasing and deploys AI across agencies to save $40M-$100M, and the state's first AI Steering Committee is delivering governance and procurement guidance in 2026. For Dell, the center of gravity is AI infrastructure (PowerEdge, networking, services) plus a real consolidation/standardization opening as ADOA-ASET centralizes spend.

Where Dell can play
  • AI-infrastructure / 'AI factory' demand tied to the TSMC semiconductor megaproject and a top-tier data-center market (PowerEdge, networking, services).
  • IT consolidation and standardization under the ACE Initiative — a chance to displace fragmented agency buying with standardized Dell platforms and lifecycle.
  • Governed agency AI adoption as the AI Steering Committee lands procurement guidance — private/on-prem AI for sensitive state workloads.
  • Endpoint, client, and managed-services refresh across 130+ agencies served by ADOA-ASET.

Budget & Fiscal

Arizona budgets on an annual fiscal-year cycle (July 1-June 30). Governor Hobbs signed an $18.3B bipartisan FY2027 budget on June 13, 2026 (effective July 1, 2026), keeping spending growth to ~3% with a three-year data-center incentive moratorium and ~$1.4B in tax cuts. There is no single published 'state IT budget' line; technology spend is distributed across agencies, with ADOA-ASET providing centralized enterprise technology services and the ACE Initiative now consolidating purchasing. The state's most consequential tech dollars sit outside the appropriations bill — in the private semiconductor and data-center buildout it is courting and, increasingly, managing.

Initiatives & Policy

TSMC Phoenix Semiconductor Megaproject4

Accelerating

TSMC's North Phoenix campus has expanded from $12B to a committed ~$165B — planned for six wafer fabs, two advanced-packaging (CoWoS) facilities, and an R&D center, the largest foreign greenfield investment in U.S. history. Fab 1 is in volume production (4nm) and has swung to profitability; the second fab finished construction in April 2026 (volume production expected 2H2027); the board approved buying another 900 acres at a January 2026 state land auction; and in May 2026 the board approved a further ~$20B capital injection from the Taiwan parent to keep the North Phoenix expansion on track (reports suggest the footprint could eventually grow toward 12 fabs). Headwinds persist — water supply, H-1B labor costs, and migrating Taiwanese suppliers. This anchors an AI-hardware supply chain in-state and is the strongest tailwind for Dell ISG and AI-infrastructure demand around it.

Arizona Data-Center Buildout9

Booming (being managed)

Arizona is a top-two U.S. data-center market alongside Texas, with Google, Microsoft, Meta, and Apple expanding around Phoenix and multi-gigawatt projects landing (the ~1.5GW Hassayampa Ranch). The state is now actively managing grid and water pressure: the FY2027 budget enacted a three-year pause on data-center tax incentives (no new ACA exemption applications July 1, 2026-June 30, 2029), Hobbs' April 2026 strategic energy plan warned data-center loads could require up to ~29,000 MW of new power, and community opposition forced the once-largest proposed project — La Osa near Eloy (Pinal County) — to be scaled back ~80% (from 59 buildings/~3,300 acres to ~11 buildings capped at ~1GW; a county revisit is set for Aug 26, 2026). Net: growth continues but with higher costs, longer timelines, and stricter siting — underlying demand for compute, networking, and services remains very strong.

ACE — Arizona Capacity & Efficiency Initiative6

Launched (Mar 2026)

The state explicitly cites agencies independently buying similar IT tools with unused seats and duplication — a direct opening to standardize on Dell platforms and lifecycle across the enterprise.

Procurement & Competition

The Arizona Department of Administration (ADOA) State Procurement Office (SPO) is the central procurement authority. Most state and public-sector technology buys run through SPO statewide contracts and the Arizona State Procurement Cooperative Program, which gives eligible local governments, tribes, school districts, and other public bodies shared access to competitively sourced agreements (900+ statewide contracts). ADOA-ASET (Arizona Strategic Enterprise Technology) sets statewide IT policy and delivers shared enterprise technology, data-center, and security services.

Competitive landscape

Arizona is a competitive, contract-driven market with incumbents holding SPO statewide agreements across compute, networking, and services.
