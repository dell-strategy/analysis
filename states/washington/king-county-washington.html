Overview

King County is the most populous county in Washington — roughly 2.3 million residents across Seattle and its surrounding cities — and one of the largest local governments in the Pacific Northwest. Its enterprise technology is consolidated under King County Information Technology (KCIT), a central IT department that delivers infrastructure, applications, data, and security services to more than 15,000 county employees across all departments. KCIT reports a biennium budget on the order of $377 million and a workforce of roughly 450 technology professionals, making it a substantial buying center in its own right rather than a thin coordinating office. The county is in a leadership transition at the top: Girmay Zahilay was sworn in as County Executive in November 2025 (the youngest in county history), succeeding acting executive Shannon Braddock, whose proposed 2026–2027 biennial budget framed the county's fiscal posture. King County is best understood as a large, self-directed enterprise buyer that purchases through Washington's cooperative vehicles (DES master contracts and NASPO ValuePoint) plus its own competitive solicitations — and that also sponsors cooperative agreements other jurisdictions can ride. Budget & Fiscal

King County is a large direct spender, not a pass-through. The county's adopted 2026–2027 biennial budget runs in the ~$20 billion range (the proposed plan was $19.7B; the adopted figure was reported higher, near $20.16B), funding services for ~2.4M residents and more than 18,000 employees. Within that, KCIT reports a biennium budget on the order of $377M. Confirm enacted totals against King County's adopted budget book before forecasting.

Initiatives & Priorities

Strategic IT Plan 2024-2027 & technical-debt reduction

KCIT's current plan spans 56 strategic initiatives across five goals, centered on modernizing aging legacy equipment ('technical debt'), retiring redundant applications, and a new IT investment-prioritization model (enterprise-architecture portfolio tool) targeted for full operation in early 2026. A durable pull for infrastructure refresh, hybrid cloud, and application rationalization.

AI Innovation Fund & generative-AI rollout

KCIT proposed an Artificial Intelligence Innovation Fund (~$1M reallocated balance) with agency proposals due early 2026, routed through its existing technology project-review process; Microsoft Copilot license requests go through the same gate. The county's GenAI guidelines (2024) set responsible-use expectations and a software-request review path. Policy is still forming — the gate on what AI offerings are buyable.

Information-security investment

KCIT has flagged significant new information-security investment, citing the wave of ransomware and attacks on neighboring public entities (e.g., Port of Seattle, Seattle Public Library). Expect continued demand for detection, response, identity, and resilience across a large, distributed county environment.
