Rhode Island is one of the most centralized technology buyers in the country: a single division (the Division of Enterprise Technology Strategy & Services, ETSS, under the Department of Administration) sets IT strategy for the executive branch, and a single Division of Purchases runs statewide Master Price Agreements through the Ocean State Procures (OSP) eProcurement system. Three dynamics define the current moment for a Dell seller. First, ETSS is in a heavy modernization push — it completed 114 projects in 2025 (up from 46), including a Workday-based ERP cutover that retired aging mainframe systems. Second, the 2024 RIBridges data breach triggered a sustained security rebuild (Splunk, firewall, and VPN upgrades) that keeps cybersecurity at the top of the agenda. Third, McKee's AI Task Force released its statewide AI roadmap in January 2026, and ETSS is rolling out Microsoft Copilot Chat across executive agencies. The buying motion is simple to map but contract-gated — Dell coverage on the right MPAs and registration in OSP is the price of entry.

Where Dell can play
  • Infrastructure and endpoint refresh tied to ETSS modernization (ERP cutover retired mainframe systems; ~4,500 PCs configured in 2025).
  • Cybersecurity rebuild after the RIBridges breach — secure infrastructure, logging/SIEM-adjacent compute, and managed services.
  • AI enablement as the state operationalizes its Jan 2026 roadmap and the DAAI Center of Excellence — governed, private AI for sensitive state data.
  • Higher-ed and research compute at URI, Brown, and RIC's new cybersecurity institute as independent AI-infrastructure buyers.

Budget & Fiscal

Rhode Island budgets annually; the fiscal year begins July 1. Governor McKee signed a $15.2B FY2027 budget on June 12, 2026 — the largest in state history, roughly $300M above his January proposal and ~$900M above FY2026. As in most states, there is no single consolidated 'state IT budget' line — technology spend sits inside ETSS operations and is distributed across agency programs. ETSS itself manages a large contract portfolio: it reported roughly $154M in contract value and ~$5.3M in negotiated vendor savings in 2025.

Procurement & Competition

Rhode Island runs a tightly centralized model. The Division of Enterprise Technology Strategy & Services (ETSS) — within the Department of Administration — owns executive-branch IT strategy, operations, and most technology projects. The Division of Purchases (RIDOP) is the statewide procurement authority; technology buys typically run through Master Price Agreements (MPAs) competed for the whole state, with many open to municipalities, higher-ed, and quasi-public entities. All solicitations and awards flow through the Ocean State Procures (OSP) eProcurement platform.

Competitive landscape

Rhode Island is a small, centralized, relationship-driven market. With one IT division and one purchasing office, deals hinge on being on the right MPAs and aligning to ETSS's active priorities — modernization, post-breach security, and AI enablement — rather than competing purely on price. Incumbents with established MPA coverage have an edge; differentiation comes from secure infrastructure, AI-ready compute, and services that map to ETSS's published roadmap.
