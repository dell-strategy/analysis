Overview

The Virginia Information Technologies Agency (VITA) is the commonwealth's central IT organization, sitting under the Secretary of Administration in the governor's cabinet. VITA sets statewide technology and security policy, owns the IT procurement vehicles executive-branch agencies buy through, and delivers shared infrastructure (data center and storage, network, end-user computing, messaging, mainframe, and public cloud). Per VITA's own figures, the agency supports roughly 65,000 users and 2,500 applications and oversees on the order of $1.3 billion in annual technology procurement. VITA is notable for its multisourcing model — one of the earliest in the United States — under which infrastructure services are split across multiple specialized suppliers and coordinated by a Multisourcing Service Integrator (MSI). For a vendor, VITA is best understood as both the standards-setter and the contracting path into Virginia's executive branch rather than a single large end-buyer; understanding which supplier owns a given tower, and where VITA's statewide contracts apply, is the key to the motion.

Initiatives & Priorities

Multisourcing model evolution

VITA splits infrastructure across specialized suppliers (data center, network, security, end-user, messaging, mainframe, public cloud) coordinated by an MSI. Re-procurements and tower refreshes are the recurring openings — know which supplier owns each tower before forecasting.

Cloud and AI modernization

CIO Mike Watson's mandate explicitly covers enterprise IT, cloud transformation, AI adoption, and digital modernization across the commonwealth's portfolio — a stated priority that opens infrastructure and platform conversations.

Statewide security program

VITA owns commonwealth security standards and the managed-security tower, and hosts the annual Commonwealth Information Security Conference. Security posture and managed-security delivery are an active, centrally governed track.

Cybersecurity
Data-center policy environment

Virginia hosts the nation's largest commercial data-center market, and the Spanberger administration has signaled data centers should 'pay their fair share' while honoring signed contracts. State-side data-center and AI-infrastructure policy is politically active and worth tracking for downstream demand and constraints.
