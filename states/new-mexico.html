New Mexico is two markets at once. The first is federal-scale HPC: Sandia and Los Alamos national labs run some of the world's most advanced supercomputers (Los Alamos' Venado, Sandia's new Spectra) and partner across the NNSA tri-lab program — a sustained, high-end compute and AI buyer that sits in-state. The second is a brand-new commercial AI build-out: Project Jupiter in Santa Teresa, a multi-building Oracle/OpenAI Stargate campus announced in February 2026, plus Intel's $3.5B advanced-packaging fabs (Fab 9 / Fab 11x) in Rio Rancho. The state itself budgets annually; the FY2027 budget is ~$33.4B with the Department of Information Technology (DoIT) funded around $113M for enterprise services, cybersecurity, and broadband. For Dell sellers, the headline angle is national-lab and research HPC/AI-infrastructure, layered on a steady state-government refresh. Note the timing: Gov. Lujan Grisham is term-limited and 2026 is a gubernatorial election year, so a DoIT/CIO leadership transition is likely after January 2027.

Where Dell can play
  • National-lab and research HPC/AI-infrastructure pull — Sandia, Los Alamos, UNM, and NM Tech are sophisticated, high-end compute buyers in-state.
  • Commercial AI build-out (Project Jupiter / Stargate, Intel Rio Rancho) creating an ISG and AI-factory ecosystem and partner motion in southern and central New Mexico.
  • State-government modernization under DoIT — enterprise services, cybersecurity, and endpoint/lifecycle refresh on an annual budget.
  • Emerging-tech and broadband money ($270M+ for fusion/quantum in 2026; ~$675M federal BEAD) seeding new compute and connectivity demand.

Dell Action Plan

  • Lead with HPC/AI-infrastructure into the national labs and research universities — Sandia, Los Alamos, UNM, NM Tech, NMSU — where the highest-end, recurring compute demand sits; engage through lab integrators and DOE/NNSA programs.
  • Build a partner/ISG motion around the commercial AI build-out (Project Jupiter / Stargate in Santa Teresa, Intel Rio Rancho) — supplier, edge, and adjacent-account opportunities orbit these anchors.

  • Time state-government engagement around the leadership transition — Lujan Grisham is term-limited and a new administration plus likely DoIT/CIO change arrives January 2027; build relationships now, expect priorities to reset.
  • Position governed, private/on-prem AI for sensitive state workloads as New Mexico debates AI guardrails and agencies modernize under DoIT.

Budget & Fiscal

New Mexico budgets on an annual fiscal cycle (FY begins July 1). The 2026 legislative session enacted an FY2027 budget of roughly $33.4B, including more than $11B from the general fund, and directed over $500M to economic development — with $270M+ aimed at emerging technologies like fusion and quantum. There is no single consolidated 'state IT budget'; technology spend is distributed across agencies, with the Department of Information Technology (DoIT) funded around $113M for compliance, project management, enterprise services, broadband access, cybersecurity, and program support.

Procurement & Competition

Central state procurement runs through the State Purchasing Division (SPD) within the General Services Department (GSD), which enforces the State Procurement Code and establishes statewide price agreements usable by state agencies and other New Mexico governmental entities. For technology, New Mexico leans heavily on cooperative vehicles — notably NASPO ValuePoint — and has historically purchased Dell through that channel. DoIT sets statewide IT policy, security standards, and enterprise services.

Competitive landscape

Two distinct competitive fields. State-government IT is cooperative-contract driven and price-sensitive, with oversight concerns historically flagged around ad-hoc purchasing — coverage and compliance matter. The lab and research-HPC field is a high-end, integrator-led arena where differentiation comes from AI-infrastructure capability, references, and services rather than catalog price.
